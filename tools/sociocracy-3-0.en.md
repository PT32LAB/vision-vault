Sociocracy 3.0 (usually written **S3**) is a free, openly-licensed (CC BY-SA 4.0) library of governance and collaboration **patterns** — small, reusable practices a group can adopt one at a time to make decisions and distribute authority without a boss at the centre. It was created in 2015 by Bernhard Bockelbrink, James Priest, and Liliana David, who took the older "Sociocracy" tradition (consent-based circles, dating to the 1970s Netherlands) and re-packaged it as a modular toolkit rather than an all-or-nothing system. The whole thing is documented in *A Practical Guide to Sociocracy 3.0*, translated into several languages.

The one idea to take away: **S3 is not a system you install — it's a menu you pull from.** You find a specific pain point, pick the one pattern that addresses it, try it, keep it if it helps. That "pull, don't push" stance is what makes it different from heavier frameworks.

## What problem it solves

Most groups make decisions in one of two broken ways: a few people decide for everyone (fast but resentful), or everyone has to agree on everything (fair but paralysing). S3's core move is **consent decision-making**: a proposal goes ahead *unless someone raises a valid objection* — a specific, reasoned concern that the proposal would cause harm or hinder the group's goals. That's the crucial distinction:

- **Consensus** asks "is everyone *for* this?" → unanimity required → slow.
- **Consent** asks "is anyone *against* this, with a reason?" → "good enough for now, safe enough to try" → fast, and revisable.

A mere preference ("I'd have done it differently") is not an objection. This single rule is why S3 groups can move quickly without steamrolling anyone.

## The core vocabulary (plain-language)

| Term | What it actually means |
|------|------------------------|
| **Driver** | The reason you're acting — a situation that needs a response. S3 trains you to name the driver *before* proposing solutions. |
| **Consent** | A decision stands if there are no valid objections (vs. consensus = everyone must actively agree). |
| **Objection** | A specific argued reason a decision would cause harm or impede the goal — not a personal preference. |
| **Circle** | A self-governing, semi-autonomous team responsible for a **domain** (an area of work + the authority to decide about it). |
| **Role** | A named responsibility with defined accountabilities, held by a person inside a circle. |
| **Single / double linking** | How circles connect: one shared member (single) or two (double — info flows both ways, classic sociocracy's signature). |
| **Navigating via tension** | "Tension" = the felt gap between what is and what could be. You surface tensions and turn the worthwhile ones into drivers to act on. |
| **Proposal forming** | A structured way to co-create a proposal from a driver before taking it to a consent decision. |
| **Pull adoption** | Adopt patterns only when a real need pulls them in — never roll out the whole framework top-down. |

## The seven principles

Every pattern is an expression of these: **Effectiveness, Consent, Empiricism, Continuous Improvement, Equivalence** (those affected by a decision get a say in it), **Transparency**, and **Accountability**.

## How a group actually starts

1. Pick one nagging problem (a driver).
2. Adopt the single pattern that fits — often *consent decision-making* in your existing meetings, or *clarifying a domain* for a confused team.
3. Run it as an experiment; review with consent; keep, adapt, or drop.
4. Let demand pull in the next pattern. There is no "go live" date.

Because it's pattern-based and compatible with agile/Kanban/Teal/Scrum, S3 layers onto whatever a group already does instead of replacing it — which is exactly why it's lower-risk than Holacchy-style "constitutions" that demand wholesale conversion.

## How it relates to neighbours

- **Classic Sociocracy** (Sociocracy For All, etc.) — same DNA (consent, circles, double-linking) but delivered as a defined method; S3 unbundles it into optional patterns.
- **Holacracy** — also descends from sociocracy, but is a single trademarked constitution you adopt whole. S3 is the open, à-la-carte alternative.
- **Reinventing Organizations** (Laloux) & the **Advice Process** — compatible self-management ideas; S3 supplies concrete mechanics they leave abstract.

## Why it matters for the project

A semi-autonomous Coroico community needs a way to make binding decisions among people who arrive as near-strangers, without recreating either a strongman or endless circular meetings. S3 fits the vault's *total pragmatism* value unusually well:

1. **Incremental, low-commitment.** You can start with one pattern (consent decisions in the founding circle) and grow only as needed — appropriate for a `status: seed` community that doesn't yet know its own shape.
2. **Free and forkable.** CC BY-SA licensing means BARK can copy, translate (Spanish exists), and adapt the patterns without permission or cost.
3. **Pairs with the relational layer.** S3 handles *how we decide*; the trust/relational practices in [[community-weaving-toolkit]] handle *whether we can stand each other first*. The article that surfaced this ([[animate-intelligence]]) makes exactly that point: "trust comes before clarity."
4. **Composable with our other governance refs.** Consent decisions inside circles + [[polis]] for large-scale opinion-mapping + [[evolution-of-trust]] as the explainer for *why* cooperation needs structure = a coherent governance stack.

## Limitations / tradeoffs

- **Vocabulary tax.** Driver/domain/consent/objection is jargon; groups need a shared glossary or it becomes cargo-cult ritual.
- **Discipline-dependent.** Consent only works if people genuinely distinguish objections from preferences — without facilitation it degrades into either consensus or rubber-stamping.
- **Governance, not magic.** S3 structures decisions; it does not create the trust, shared purpose, or conflict-tolerance those decisions depend on. It is necessary, not sufficient.
- **Best for engaged groups.** Like all participatory systems, it assumes members want to participate; it's poorly suited to passive or transactional memberships.
