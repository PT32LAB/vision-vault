# Comparative Governance Models

**6 governance models studied.** Pattern: consensus + rotating leadership works. Token-only governance doesn't. Mondragon (70,000 members, cooperative) thrives. CityDAO (token-based, 40 acres) hit plutocracy and 1-5% participation. Bolivia's ayllu system, tested over centuries, outperforms Silicon Valley's latest experiment.

---

BARK's governance model isn't decided yet -- and that's deliberate. We believe the community should choose how it governs itself. Here are the models we're exploring.

## 01 -- Cooperative Model

Traditional cooperative structure with one-member-one-vote democracy. Legally recognized in Bolivia. Proven track record across centuries and cultures.

- **Pros:** Legal clarity, institutional recognition, democratic, well-understood
- **Cons:** Can be slow to adapt, geographic constraints on membership, traditional governance overhead

## 02 -- DAO Model

Decentralized Autonomous Organization with on-chain governance. Token-based voting, transparent treasury management, smart contract enforcement of decisions.

- **Pros:** Transparent, enables global participation, programmable rules, resistant to capture
- **Cons:** Legal gray area in Bolivia, technical barrier to participation, plutocratic risk (more tokens = more votes)

## 03 -- Hybrid Approach

Legal cooperative for real-world assets and local operations. DAO layer for community decisions, remote participation, and treasury governance. The cooperative owns the land and physical infrastructure; the DAO coordinates the community's direction and resource allocation.

- **Pros:** Legal protection + digital participation, best of both worlds
- **Cons:** Complexity, potential conflicts between layers, requires careful design

## Other Models Under Study

- **Sociocracy** -- consent-based decision-making with nested circles of authority
- **Ayllu system** -- traditional Andean communal governance, deeply relevant to the Bolivian context. See [[glossary]]
- **Holacracy** -- distributed authority through self-organizing roles
- **Liquid democracy** -- delegative voting where you can vote directly or delegate your vote to trusted representatives

## Lessons from the Real World

Theory is cheap. Here are real governance experiments -- some spanning decades, some already failed -- and what they actually teach about governing a physical community.

### Mondragon Corporation (Basque Country, Spain)

The world's largest cooperative: 70,000+ employees, over 11 billion EUR in revenue (as of [2023 annual report](https://www.mondragon-corporation.com/en/about-us/)). One-member-one-vote governance with elected management, even at massive scale. Mondragon proves that cooperatives are not limited to small artisan workshops -- they can compete with multinational corporations while maintaining democratic governance. [mondragon-corporation.com](https://www.mondragon-corporation.com/)

### Auroville (Tamil Nadu, India)

Over 3,000 residents from 60 nations, founded in 1968. A cautionary tale: in 2022, the Indian government's Auroville Foundation overrode resident self-governance, unilaterally pushing development plans against community consensus. Decades of community building can be undermined by external authority in a single administrative action. **Lesson:** legal structure matters as much as internal governance. [auroville.org](https://auroville.org/)

### Zapatista Autonomous Municipalities (Chiapas, Mexico)

Over 30 years of "mandar obedeciendo" (lead by obeying). Rotating leadership, community assemblies, governance structures spanning dozens of autonomous municipalities across Chiapas. In 2023, the Zapatistas restructured their governance due to external pressures -- cartel violence, youth emigration, economic stress. Shows that even successful governance must adapt continuously; no system is set-and-forget.

### Tamera (Portugal)

170 residents using a consent-based "Forum" process for decisions. Daily community check-ins where any member can raise issues. Works well at small scale for internal cohesion, but raises insularity concerns -- the community can become an echo chamber without deliberate outside engagement.

### The Ayllu (Bolivia/Peru)

Indigenous governance directly relevant to BARK's location. Based on ayni (reciprocity), minka (communal labor), rotating leadership, and consensus. Constitutionally recognized in Bolivia since 2009 under the plurinational state framework. This is not optional historical context -- it is the living governance reality of our actual neighbors. Any governance model BARK adopts must be compatible with and respectful of ayllu principles.

### CityDAO

A DAO that bought 40 acres in Wyoming to test on-chain governance for a physical community. The result: token-based voting led to plutocracy (more tokens = more power), governance participation hovered at 1-5%, and the project hit regulatory walls it hadn't anticipated. **Lesson:** on-chain governance alone does not work for physical communities where people share land, water, and daily life.

---

> The pattern is clear: successful long-term communities use consensus-based governance with rotating leadership. Token-based DAO governance alone has not sustained physical communities. Bolivia's ayllu system, tested over centuries, may be a better model than Silicon Valley's latest experiment.
