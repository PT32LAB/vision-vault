Polis is open-source software (maintained by The Computational Democracy Project) that runs at-scale deliberations: participants submit short statements, vote agree/disagree/pass on each other's, and the system clusters opinion groups via PCA and surfaces *bridging* statements — points where the largest cross-group majority exists. Famous for its role in Taiwan's vTaiwan process, where it helped resolve contested policy on Uber, alcohol regulation, and others by finding rough consensus rather than forcing majority votes.

Why it matters as a piece of design:
- **Wikisurvey, not poll.** Participants generate the question space, not just the answers.
- **Optimizes for bridges, not battles.** The visualization rewards finding what unites groups rather than amplifying division.
- **Genuinely scales.** Used with groups from 40 to 40,000.
- **Open source.** Self-hostable, auditable, free.

## Why it matters for the project

Polis is the most directly *useful* tool on this list — not just inspiration but a piece of governance infrastructure we could deploy in BARK and the wider NetworkTribes ecosystem. Three uses:
1. **Live tool for community decisions.** Once the Coroico community grows past the size where everyone-in-a-room works, Polis is a serious candidate for distributed deliberation.
2. **Vision-page demo.** Embedding a small live Polis on a vault page (e.g., "What does autonomy mean to you?") would let visitors actually participate in the project rather than read about it.
3. **Argument anchor.** When we make the case that current voting systems are not the only option, Polis + vTaiwan is a citeable working alternative.

**10 / 10** — Working open-source infrastructure with proven national-scale deployments, directly applicable to NetworkTribes governance. The single most operationally relevant link in the list.
