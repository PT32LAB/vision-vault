Nicky Case's *The Evolution of Trust* walks the reader through the iterated Prisoner's Dilemma in fifteen minutes of playable pages. You play rounds against different bot strategies, watch tournaments between them, then experiment with payoffs and noise. By the end, you've internalized the conditions under which trust can stably evolve: repeated interactions, clear payoffs, low miscommunication, and the courage to forgive once after defection. Translated into 40+ languages by volunteers.

What it does well:
- **Story over lecture.** A friendly narrator walks beside you; you never feel taught at.
- **Play-then-name.** The mechanics teach the intuition first; technical names come after.
- **Honest tradeoffs.** It does not flatter the reader — it shows when cooperation breaks and why.

## Why it matters for the project

Three uses, in increasing ambition:
1. **Linkable explainer.** Drop it into any page about local governance, cooperatives, or community decision-making — saves us from rebuilding the argument.
2. **Format template.** This is the canonical example of how to teach a structural truth in fifteen minutes without losing the reader. The playable-page-flow is reusable for our own content (e.g., "How a regenerative economy emerges").
3. **Diagnostic frame.** The conditions for trust (repetition, clarity, low noise, forgiveness) become a literal checklist for designing community structures in BARK.

## Rating

**10 / 10** — Iconic and directly applicable. It is the single best public artifact for understanding why local-scale, repeated-interaction communities can evolve cooperation that nation-states can't.
