Low-Tech Magazine has been published since 2007 by the Belgian science-and-technology journalist Kris De Decker, working from Spain. Its premise: that a serious portion of the energy and material problems we frame as needing high-tech fixes have already been solved — often centuries ago — by tools, methods, and infrastructures that we have abandoned. The magazine reconstructs those solutions, measures them, and presents them as live design options rather than nostalgia.

Distinctive moves:
- **Solar-powered, self-hosted website.** The site runs on a server fed by a small photovoltaic panel and a battery, and goes offline when the weather is bad — a public real-time demonstration of the editorial argument.
- **Engineered scarcity in the medium itself.** Pages are static, dithered images replace photographs, no ads, no analytics; the home page is around 500 KiB. The publication's *form* embodies its *thesis*.
- **No techno-optimism, no collapse-porn.** The frame is "what worked, why it stopped, and what would it cost to bring it back" — appliances, heating, cooling, transport, storage, agriculture.
- **Published in 12 languages** (EN/FR/DE/NL/ES/IT/PT/PL/AR/VI/KO/TR), with offline-readable book compilations of the back catalogue.

## Why it matters for the project

This publication is a near-perfect intellectual partner for the technology-sovereignty cluster, and useful in three concrete ways:

1. **Component sourcing.** Many of the pieces under consideration for a semi-autonomous Coroico site (solar food drying, passive cooling, hand-tool workshops, fireless cookers, low-voltage DC lighting, human-powered machines) have already been engineered and field-tested in the magazine's archive. It is a reusable parts catalogue, not just an inspiration.
2. **Communication template.** The site is one of the strongest existing examples of how to make low-energy, repairable, place-based technology *legible and desirable* to a global audience without slipping into either eco-austerity or solarpunk fantasy. Tone, layout, and the "the medium is the demonstration" trick are all directly transferable.
3. **Audience overlap.** Readers of Low-Tech Magazine are pre-qualified for BARK's framing — they already accept that complexity and energy throughput are the variables to reduce, not optimise.

## Limitations

- The archive is heavily Northern-European and North-American in its case material; Latin-American and tropical analogues mostly need to be sourced elsewhere.
- The publication is editorial, not operational — it documents working low-tech systems but does not run a community deploying them at scale. Useful as a library, not as a partner organisation.
- The "sometimes offline" model is a feature for them and a bug for anyone trying to cite it from a deadline; mirror or archive anything load-bearing.

## Rating

**9 / 10** — for the technology-sovereignty cluster specifically, this is one of the highest-value external references in the vault. The combination of depth, longevity, multilingual reach, and the medium-is-the-message demonstration is rare.
