The Pudding's November 2025 piece walks the reader through every Congressional speech mentioning "democracy" since 1880, classified by an LLM into who is being framed as the threat. Bright dots = "democracy is under attack" speeches; the reader scrubs through history and watches Southern Democrats, Cold-War conservatives, civil-rights opponents, and contemporary partisans all reach for the same word against very different enemies.

The format is the argument: an interactive scatterplot fused with a personal essay. The data carries the rigor; the prose carries the stakes ("If we stop now, who are we as a people?").

## Why it matters for the project

Pudding pieces are the gold standard for explaining politically charged systems without preaching. Three things to steal:
1. **Long historical baselines** — showing a 145-year arc disarms the "this time is different" reflex on either side.
2. **Reader-as-investigator framing** — the dots are queryable, so the conclusion feels earned rather than asserted.
3. **First-person closing** — data journalism that ends with "what now?" rather than "the end."

For BARK and the wider vision, this is the template for any piece arguing that current institutions are failing on their own terms — let the historical record speak first, then ask the reader where they want to stand.

## Rating

**8 / 10** — Outstanding craft and directly relevant tone. Slightly U.S.-specific, so we'd be borrowing technique rather than substance.
