Richard Hanania's May 2026 essay argues that Latin America's extreme homicide rates are not a cultural accident or an income-level inevitability, but a specific governance trap: **middle-income democracies inherit civil liberties frameworks they cannot afford to enforce.** Wealthy democracies can fund the courts, prisons, and police required to make criminal protections work. Authoritarian states bypass those protections entirely. Latin American democracies sit in between — formal rights on paper, clearance rates in the 10–20% range in practice.

The piece is worth tracking for the BARK project because it quantifies what "rule of law failure" looks like at the regional scale, and it names the governance mechanism rather than retreating to culture or poverty as explanations.

## Core argument

Hanania runs a regression on global homicide rates controlling for GDP. Non-democracies scatter around the expected line. Middle-income democracies — especially in Latin America — cluster far above it. Brazil, Colombia, and Mexico post homicide rates comparable to China, Azerbaijan, and Georgia despite being significantly wealthier.

His explanation: **democracy as a package includes criminal procedural rights.** Those rights are expensive to honour. When state capacity is thin — stretched courts, underpaid police, overcrowded prisons — the rights remain on paper while enforcement collapses. Gang leaders run prison empires in El Salvador and Brazil that would be impossible under authoritarian control because the system cannot actually contain them.

## Key data points

- **Brazil:** 11.7 homicides per 100,000 in 1980 (military rule), rising through democratization to 28.9 in 2003; a 50% wealthier Brazil now than 1985 but persistently elevated rates
- **Russia:** Homicide peaked at ~32 per 100,000 in 1994 during early democratization, fell as authoritarianism returned
- **Mexico/Honduras:** Murder clearance rates 10–20%, versus 50–60% in the U.S.
- **Latin America:** Slowest-growing world region 2010–2020 — violence extracts real economic cost

## El Salvador as natural experiment

Hanania treats Bukele's mass incarceration campaign as evidence that the criminal-rights tradeoff is real. Dramatic drops in extortion and murder followed the crackdown. Bukele's 90%+ approval rating is presented as the population correctly identifying the tradeoff: they prefer order to procedural purity when the state cannot deliver both.

He does not endorse this as a model — he presents it as a data point for understanding why strongman politics gains traction in genuinely failed states.

## The "unbundling" proposal

The essay closes with a speculative frame: democracy could be **unbundled** — maintaining elections and press freedom while adopting stricter crime-control approaches. Western insistence on the complete democratic package (elections + civil liberties + criminal protections) may discredit democracy itself by associating it with impunity.

This is the most contested part of the argument. The counterargument (noted by Scott Sumner in comments) is that Latin American culture may explain the variance independently of regime type, and that democracies typically outperform authoritarian systems economically over time.

## Why this matters for the project

1. **Regional context for Bolivia.** The BARK project operates in a country with one of the lower homicide rates in Latin America, but the regional governance trap Hanania describes — thin state capacity, weak enforcement — applies. Understanding why the region looks the way it does is prerequisite to honest resilience planning.

2. **Safety as a governance variable, not a cultural given.** Hanania shows that violence tracks governance design, not ethnicity or culture. That framing is useful when making the case that intentional community design can shift safety outcomes — the level of order is not fixed by geography.

3. **The tradeoff is real.** If a semi-autonomous community must rely on state enforcement as backstop, the clearance-rate data (10–20%) should inform how much weight to place on that backstop. Internal dispute resolution and community-level accountability structures matter more in a low-clearance environment than in one where external law enforcement is reliable.

4. **Investor/partner context.** Anyone evaluating the BARK project from outside Latin America will likely hold pessimistic priors about regional safety. This essay provides a structural explanation (governance + state capacity) rather than a fatalistic one, and points to levers that communities can influence at their own scale.

## Limitations

- **Single author, political tilt.** Hanania writes from a heterodox-right perspective; some arguments shade toward justifying authoritarian order. Read the data carefully, hold the policy conclusions loosely.
- **El Salvador is one data point.** The Bukele experiment is recent and the long-run effects (institutional erosion, recidivism, economic effects) are not yet visible.
- **Bolivia is not in the high-homicide cohort.** Bolivia's homicide rate (~5–7 per 100,000) is far below Brazil or Honduras — the worst-case regional picture in this essay is not Bolivia's current situation.
- **Culture hypothesis not fully rebutted.** Sumner's challenge stands: the regression does not fully separate governance from shared cultural or historical factors.
