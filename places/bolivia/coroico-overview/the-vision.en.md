# The Vision

A community in the Bolivian cloud forest, learning to provision its own power, food, shelter, and governance -- and to keep doing so when the external systems it once depended on stop being reliable.

![Coroico in morning cloud](/images/yungas/coroico-clouds.jpg)

BARK -- the Bolivian Ark -- is taking root at 1,750 meters in the Yungas, the subtropical crease where the Andes collapse into the Amazon basin. The name matters. An ark is not a bunker. It carries living things through rough water and sets them down somewhere they can keep living. Bunkers entomb. Arks travel.

## A Community, Not a Compound

Compounds store supplies behind walls. Communities produce what they need. BARK runs on five linked components:

- A **fabrication lab and [[technopark|techno park]]** where broken water pumps get repaired, circuit boards get milled, and appropriate technology gets documented in the open
- A **startup [[incubator]]** that launches ventures rooted in Coroico's actual soil and actual needs
- A **[[trading|trading house]]** that moves Yungas coffee, cacao, and craft goods through channels the growers built themselves
- A **[[retreat|retreat center]]** where Aymara healers and biosensor engineers sit in the same room, comparing notes on what the body is doing during ceremony
- The **[[infrastructure]] layer** underneath -- solar panels, spring-fed cisterns, vertical growing walls, mesh radios -- that makes autonomy a material fact, not an aspiration on a slide deck

> A maker space without its own power grid is a hobby. A retreat center that cannot feed its guests is a resort with incense.

No single component here is unprecedented. Solar works. Permaculture works. Cooperatives work. The ambition is in the connections between them -- shared power, shared data, shared labor pools that let each component compensate when another struggles. When the road to La Paz washes out in the February rains, the community keeps eating, keeps communicating, keeps working.

## Open Source as Survival Strategy

Every solar array schematic, every governance vote, every agricultural trial result gets published. This is practical, not altruistic. Open-source projects attract better collaborators, survive their founders, and resist capture by any single faction. If BARK works, the plans should spread. One community in one valley is an experiment. Twenty communities across three continents is a proof of concept.

## Embedded, Not Parachuted In

Coroico has existed since before the Spanish arrived. The terraced slopes above town carry a thousand years of agricultural intelligence in their contour lines. This place does not need rescue. What it can use is investment that stays put, jobs that persist beyond a construction phase, and technology transfer that runs in both directions -- the fabrication lab learns from the farmer as much as the farmer learns from the lab. BARK hires locally, buys locally, and submits its governance to mechanisms that give the Coroiqueno community real leverage over what happens on their land.

## Capability, Not Stockpiles

Hoarding is a losing game. Supplies run out. Canned food expires. Skills atrophy in storage. BARK invests in living capability instead. The question is never "how many months of rice do we have in the warehouse" but "can we grow cassava, mill a replacement pump shaft, stitch a wound, and teach someone else to do all three?" Energy independence, local manufacturing, medical knowledge preserved in daily practice rather than entombed on a hard drive. A community that can repair its own systems does not need to predict which specific crisis arrives next.
