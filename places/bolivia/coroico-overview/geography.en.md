# The Location: Coroico, Yungas

Coroico and the Yungas -- where cloud forest meets civilization.

![Coroico town in morning cloud, Yungas, Bolivia](/images/yungas/coroico-clouds.jpg)
*Photo: Coroico by Claudio Laura Daza, CC BY 4.0 via Wikimedia Commons*

## Geography & Position

BARK is planned for the **Coroico area** in the Yungas region of Bolivia -- a subtropical valley on the eastern slopes of the Andes at approximately **1,750 meters elevation**. Coordinates: **16 11'S, 67 43'W**. The town sits at the transition zone between the high Altiplano and the Amazon basin, creating one of the most biologically diverse corridors on Earth.

The Yungas occupy a narrow band of cloud forest on the eastern Andean slopes, where warm, moisture-laden air from the Amazon rises and condenses. This creates a subtropical highland climate with year-round growing conditions -- the single most important factor for a self-sufficient community.

[View Coroico on Google Maps](https://www.google.com/maps/place/Coroico,+Bolivia/)

> A climate that most agronomists would design from scratch if they could: twelve-month growing season, abundant spring water, altitude that moderates tropical heat into perpetual spring.

## Climate Data

- **Mean annual temperature** -- 18-19 C. Comfortable year-round with no need for heating or cooling systems.
- **Daily highs** -- 22-28 C depending on season. Warm but never extreme.
- **Nighttime lows** -- 10-16 C. Cool sleeping, no frost risk at this elevation.
- **Annual rainfall** -- 1,100-1,300mm. A distinct wet season (November-March) provides reliable water, while the dry season (May-September) offers clear building weather.
- **Growing season** -- Year-round. No killing frosts. Multiple harvests per year for most crops.
- **Climate classification** -- Subtropical highland (Cwb under Koppen). Similar to parts of Kenya or Colombia's coffee belt.

**Year-round growing at 1,750m:** The Yungas climate allows continuous food production with no heating or cooling infrastructure needed. Coffee, cacao, citrus, avocado, banana, and hundreds of other species thrive here naturally.

## Biodiversity & Ecology

The Coroico area is adjacent to **ANMI Cotapata** (a managed conservation area), a 40,000-hectare protected area spanning multiple ecological zones from 1,000m to over 5,000m. The broader Yungas region is recognized as one of the world's biodiversity hotspots.

- **Bird species** -- [400-500+ documented in the Yungas corridor](https://datazone.birdlife.org/site/factsheet/cotapata-national-park-and-integrated-management-natural-area-iba-bolivia), including Andean cock-of-the-rock, toucans, and dozens of hummingbird species. Coroico is a known birding destination. [1]
- **Plant species** -- 3,000+ in the Cotapata-Yungas zone, including orchids, tree ferns, bromeliads, and medicinal plants used for centuries by indigenous communities. [2]
- **Wildlife** -- Spectacled bear (Tremarctos ornatus) in the protected areas and less-inhabited zones of the Yungas corridor, plus Andean fox, puma, and primate species in the lower-altitude sections of the Yungas corridor (below 1,200m).
- **Cultural heritage** -- Part of the UNESCO-listed **Qhapaq Nan** (Andean Road System). The pre-Columbian road from La Paz to the Yungas passes through this area, a route used for over 1,000 years. [3]

## Demographics & Community

The Coroico municipality had a population of approximately **19,397** in the 2012 national census, with the town center being considerably smaller. [4] The population is ethnically diverse:

- **Aymara indigenous communities** -- the predominant indigenous group in the highlands and Yungas transition zone, with deep knowledge of local agriculture and ecology
- **Afro-Bolivian communities** -- descendants of enslaved Africans brought to work in the colonial-era mines and haciendas, centered in the Yungas. A historically marginalized community with rich cultural traditions including the Saya dance. [5]
- **Languages** -- Spanish is the primary lingua franca. Aymara is widely spoken in surrounding communities. Some Quechua presence.
- **Economy** -- Tourism (domestic and international), agriculture (coffee, coca, citrus), and government services form the economic base.

## Access & Infrastructure

- **Distance from La Paz** -- 95-100 km by road. La Paz provides international airport, hospitals, government offices, and major markets.
- **Travel time** -- 3-3.5 hours by road from La Paz via the modern paved highway (opened 2006/2007), replacing the infamous "Death Road" (Yungas Road) as the primary route. [6]
- **Public transport** -- Regular minibus/bus service from La Paz (Villa Fatima terminal), costing 30-40 Bolivianos ($4-6 USD).
- **Electricity** -- Grid power available in the town and surrounding areas. Bolivia's electricity infrastructure is developing but functional in the Yungas.
- **Telecommunications** -- 3G/4G mobile coverage from Entel, Tigo, and Viva. Sufficient for basic connectivity but not high-bandwidth work.
- **Starlink** -- Bolivia [lifted restrictions on satellite internet in December 2024](https://abcnews.go.com/International/wireStory/bolivia-lifts-restrictions-satellite-internet-opening-door-spacexs-118547089), opening the door to providers like Starlink. Satellite broadband is now a viable option for high-speed connectivity independent of terrestrial infrastructure. [7]

## Natural Resources

- **Water** -- Abundant. The Coroico River and numerous mountain springs provide reliable water year-round. Multiple streams suitable for micro-hydro power generation.
- **Solar potential** -- 3.5-4.5 kWh/m2/day. Good but not exceptional -- the cloud forest means more overcast days than the Altiplano. Solar works well combined with micro-hydro for a resilient dual system.
- **Micro-hydro potential** -- Excellent. Steep terrain, reliable rainfall, and numerous streams create ideal conditions for small-scale hydroelectric generation (1-100 kW systems).
- **Fertile soil** -- Rich organic soils in the cloud forest zone. Suitable for coffee, cacao, citrus, avocado, tropical fruits, and a wide range of vegetables and herbs.
- **Gold deposits** -- The Yungas has historical and ongoing artisanal gold mining. Not a BARK priority, but relevant context for the region's resource profile.
- **Timber and bamboo** -- Sustainable forestry potential with native species. Bamboo (especially Guadua) grows rapidly and is excellent for construction.

## Safety & Security

Transparency about safety is non-negotiable. Here is what we know from verified data, travel advisories, and real accounts from people who have lived in or visited Coroico.

- **Bolivia's homicide rate: ~4.4 per 100,000** (UNODC/World Bank, 2023) -- lower than the United States (~6.4/100K) and far lower than most Latin American countries. Bolivia is among the safer countries in the region by this metric. [11]
- **Coroico is a tourist town, generally safe.** No municipality-level crime data exists (Bolivia does not publish granular data by town), but tourist forums and expat accounts consistently describe Coroico as quiet, friendly, and safe. [12]
- **US State Department: Level 2** ("Exercise Increased Caution") due to civil unrest -- the same advisory level as most of Western Europe. The Yungas is NOT designated "Do Not Travel" (that designation applies to the Chapare region). [13]
- **The coca factor.** The Yungas is a traditional coca-growing region. Critical distinction: Yungas coca is grown for legal domestic consumption (chewing, tea) -- this is culturally accepted and fully legal in Bolivia. The Chapare region is associated with coca grown for cocaine processing, which is why Chapare has a "Do Not Travel" advisory and the Yungas does not.
- **Real risks to plan for:** Road blockades during political crises (the 2024 crisis disrupted travel and supply chains across all of Bolivia), landslides during rainy season (November-March), and limited police presence in rural areas. These are logistical risks, not violent crime risks.
- **Police presence** exists but is limited in rural areas. Bolivia's UMOPAR anti-narcotics unit operates in the Yungas, but general policing is thin outside urban centers.

## What Others Say

Real experiences from people who have visited or lived in Coroico and Bolivia -- not marketing copy.

- **Expats describe Coroico as friendly, affordable, and beautiful.** One traveler stayed "twice as long as they were planning" and called it a hidden gem. Multiple outlets list Coroico in "Best Places to Retire in Bolivia" for its ideal climate, scenic beauty, and low cost of living. [14]
- **"I've been in Bolivia for about a month and had no problems. Coroico has been perfect."** -- TripAdvisor forum contributor. [12]
- **Internet connectivity is a real limitation** -- but satellite internet is changing this. Bolivia lifted restrictions on providers like Starlink in December 2024.
- **The 2024 political crisis was real.** Road blockades disrupted travel and supply chains nationwide. The tourism industry took "a serious hit." Any community in Bolivia needs a plan for political disruption and supply chain resilience -- this is why BARK's self-sufficiency architecture exists. [15]
- **"Most intentional communities fail"** -- exact failure rates are debated, but the failure patterns are well-documented: consensus without rules, vision drift, commitment gaps, economic self-deception, and single-leader dependency. BARK's governance model is designed specifically to avoid these patterns, drawing from Mondragon (68 years, EUR 11.2B revenue), Zapatista autonomous municipalities (30 years), and Bolivia's own ayllu governance tradition. [16]

## Risks & Challenges (Honest Assessment)

No location is perfect. Transparency about risks is essential for anyone considering this project seriously.

- **Landslides** -- The primary natural hazard. Steep terrain + heavy rainfall = periodic landslides, especially during the wet season (November-March). Road closures between La Paz and Coroico occur multiple times per rainy season. Serious infrastructure planning must account for slope stability. [8]
- **Rainy season road closures** -- The La Paz-Coroico road can be blocked for hours or occasionally days during major landslide events. Stockpiling essential supplies and having local self-sufficiency is not optional -- it's survival planning.
- **Seismic activity** -- Moderate. Bolivia sits on the South American tectonic plate with the Nazca plate subducting beneath. Earthquakes occur but are generally less severe than in Chile or Peru. Building codes should account for seismic loads.
- **Political stability** -- Bolivia has experienced significant political turbulence (most recently in 2019). While democratic institutions have held, political risk is real and should be factored into long-term planning. Community buy-in and legal resilience matter.
- **Local community relations** -- Critical. Any foreign-led project in Bolivia carries colonial baggage. Success requires genuine integration, local employment, shared benefits, and respect for indigenous governance traditions. This is not optional -- it's existential for the project.
- **Bureaucracy** -- Bolivian government processes can be slow and opaque. Legal work should use experienced local counsel.

## Legal Framework for BARK

- **Foreign property ownership** -- Foreigners can own property in Bolivia, with the restriction that property cannot be within 50 km of an international border. Coroico is well within safe distance. [9]
- **Cooperative law** -- Bolivia's **Ley General de Cooperativas (Ley 356, 2013)** provides a robust legal framework for cooperative organizations. A minimum of 10 members is required to form a cooperative. This aligns well with BARK's governance model. [10]
- **Community land** -- TCO (Tierras Comunitarias de Origen) laws recognize indigenous community land rights. Any land acquisition must respect existing community claims.
- **Investment climate** -- Bolivia has bilateral investment treaties with several countries. The cooperative structure provides stronger legal standing than individual foreign ownership.

## Integration, Not Colonization

We are newcomers building in a place with deep existing roots. Our legitimacy depends on earned trust, not assumed welcome. BARK's relationship with the local community is built on mutual benefit: local employment, shared infrastructure, cultural exchange, economic integration. We bring resources and skills; the community brings knowledge, culture, and belonging. This is a partnership.

The Aymara **ayllu** system -- a traditional form of communal governance and reciprocal labor -- predates European contact by centuries and offers a governance model that resonates deeply with BARK's cooperative principles. We learn from this tradition; we don't replicate it superficially.
