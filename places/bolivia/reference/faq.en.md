## The Basics

### What exactly is BARK?

BARK (Bolivian Ark) is a semi-autonomous community project in the Yungas region of Bolivia. It combines a techno park, startup incubator, trading house, and retreat center into a semi-autonomous, open-source settlement. The goal: preserve humanistic values, appropriate technology, and human diversity through a resilient, self-sustaining community.

### Where is this happening?

Near Coroico, in the Yungas valley of Bolivia. Altitude around 1,750m, subtropical climate, fertile soil, year-round water. About 3 hours from La Paz. See the [[about]] landing page for details on why this specific area.

### Who's behind this?

A small founding team -- two co-founders with backgrounds in systems architecture and knowledge design. We're not pretending to be bigger than we are.

### Is this a cult?

No. Open governance (the community chooses its own model). Open source (everything we produce is public). Voluntary participation (leave whenever you want). No charismatic leader worship -- we have co-founders, not gurus. The governance structure is designed to prevent power concentration. See [[comparative-models]] for specifics.

## Participation

### Do I need to move to Bolivia?

No. Remote participation is possible and valued -- contributing code, research, design, governance proposals, translations, and more. Some roles will eventually require on-site presence (construction, agriculture, medical), but many don't. The community is designed to function with both resident and remote members.

### What skills are most needed?

Engineering (solar, water, construction), agriculture (permaculture, tropical farming), medicine (remote healthcare, emergency), governance (cooperative law, community facilitation), education (teaching, curriculum design), and solar/communications technology. But we value diverse skills -- if you have something to contribute, we want to hear about it.

### How much does it cost to participate?

It varies. We're developing a cooperative membership structure with different tiers. For context: cost of living in Coroico is approximately $500-800/month for a comfortable life. Cooperative membership shares and investment amounts are still being defined -- we'll publish specifics once the legal structure is finalized.

### Do I need to speak Spanish?

Helpful but not required initially. The founding team communicates in English. However, integration with the local community requires Spanish (and ideally some Aymara). Language learning will be part of the community's culture -- we'll organize classes and immersion opportunities.

## Practical

### Is Bolivia safe?

Bolivia's homicide rate is approximately 4.4 per 100,000 (UNODC/World Bank, 2023) -- lower than the United States. Coroico is a tourist town with a generally safe atmosphere. Real risks are environmental, not criminal: landslides during rainy season, road closures (the road from La Paz is notorious), and altitude effects for newcomers. We take these seriously.

### What about healthcare?

Coroico has a hospital for basic care. Serious medical issues require La Paz (~3 hours by road). We're planning a community health program with telemedicine capability and basic emergency medical supplies. A medical evacuation plan is a priority for Phase 2 (infrastructure). This is a real limitation that improves over time but won't disappear.

### What about internet?

Bolivia lifted restrictions on satellite internet providers like Starlink in December 2024, making reliable broadband feasible. The town has 3G/4G coverage. We're planning a mesh network for the community site to ensure redundancy. Remote work is feasible.

### What about visas?

Visa terms vary by nationality. Many visitors receive 30-90 day tourist visas with extension options. Long-term options include: specific purpose visa (for project work), investor visa (for those putting capital in), or applying for residency. Legal counsel is required for long-term arrangements -- we'll have guidance for participants once our legal structure is in place.

## The Hard Questions

### What happens if the project fails?

Cooperative dissolution follows Bolivian law (Ley 356). Assets are distributed according to bylaws. Open-source knowledge, code, and documentation remain public regardless. Honest answer: early participants accept higher risk. This is a startup, not a guarantee. We mitigate by building real assets (land, infrastructure) and maintaining open documentation so nothing is truly lost.

### What about political risk in Bolivia?

Bolivia has experienced political instability -- coups, contested elections, social unrest. We don't minimize this. We mitigate through: legal structure that doesn't depend on any single political faction, deep local integration (so we're seen as community members, not outsiders), diversified operations that provide local employment, and maintaining international connections. See [[why-an-ark]] for more detail.

### How is this different from a rich-country colony?

Integration, not extraction. We're not arriving to take resources. We're building a cooperative that employs locally, shares infrastructure with the community, operates under Bolivian law, uses cooperative ownership (not private land grabs), and requires community consent. The [[comparative-models|governance model]] is designed to prevent the colonial pattern.

### What about the coca economy?

The Yungas region is a traditional and legal coca-growing area. Coca has deep cultural significance in Andean communities and is legally cultivated in this region. We respect local agricultural traditions and livelihoods. We're not here to change anyone's way of life -- we're here to add something complementary alongside what already exists.
