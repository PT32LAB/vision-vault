# Economy

*Macro and micro economic context for building in the Bolivian Yungas.*

---

## Bolivia Macro Overview

Bolivia is a lower-middle-income country with a resource-dependent economy and a strong state role. Understanding the national picture is essential context for any project here.

### Key Indicators

| Indicator | Value | Source |
|-----------|-------|--------|
| GDP | $44-46B | World Bank, recent years |
| GDP Per Capita | ~$3,700 | Lower-middle-income |
| Currency | BOB | Boliviano, ~6.9 per USD (managed peg) |
| Classification | Lower-Middle Income | World Bank |
| Inflation | 20%+ | 2025 |
| Active Cooperatives | 15,000 | AFCOOP |

### Economic Model

Bolivia operates a mixed economy with a strong state role and resource nationalism. Natural gas, mining (zinc, gold, tin), and agricultural exports (soybeans, quinoa) form the backbone. The government plays a direct role in resource extraction and redistribution.

The cooperative sector is constitutionally recognized -- Bolivia's State Political Constitution mentions cooperatives in 12 articles and promotes their participation in the economy. There are over 15,000 active cooperatives regulated by AFCOOP.

### Key Exports

- **Natural gas** -- historically the largest export revenue source
- **Zinc and gold** -- significant mining sector
- **Soybeans** -- primary agricultural export
- **Quinoa** -- Bolivia is historically one of the world's top producers (now rivaled by Peru)
- **Coffee** -- specialty arabica from the Yungas, growing in value

### 2024 Economic Difficulties

Bolivia experienced significant economic strain in 2024-2025: severe dollar shortages, fuel supply disruptions, and inflation exceeding 20% (up from ~2% in 2022). Foreign reserves dropped to approximately $100M, down from $14B a decade earlier (IMF, 2025; International Banker). The Boliviano's managed peg came under stress with a 70-115% gap between official and parallel exchange rates. A new pro-reform government (elected August 2025) is working with the IDB on a $4.5 billion stabilization package for 2026-2028. **Anyone building here needs to plan for economic volatility.**

## Yungas & Coroico Micro Economy

The local economy that BARK will integrate with -- smallholder agriculture, domestic tourism, and a university pipeline.

![Local market in the Yungas region of Bolivia](/images/yungas/market.jpg)

### Smallholder Farming

95% of Yungas coffee is grown by smallholders on 1-8 hectare plots. The Yungas produces nearly all of Bolivia's arabica coffee, shade-cultivated under forest canopy at 1,200-2,000m elevation.

- Coca (legal, traditional, constitutionally protected)
- Coffee (specialty arabica)
- Cacao (fine-flavor wild Beniano varieties)
- Citrus, bananas, tropical fruit

### Domestic & Backpacker Tourism

Coroico is a weekend getaway for La Paz residents and a backpacker staple. The "Death Road" mountain biking experience draws international tourists year-round.

- La Paz (3h drive) is the primary market
- Eco-lodges and hostels serve tourism
- Afro-Bolivian cultural visits from Coroico
- IDB $10M tourism development loan active

### Carmen Pampa University

A Catholic University satellite campus 12 km from Coroico with ~700 students and ~50 faculty. Cited by a UN committee as "one of the leading examples of how best to eradicate poverty."

Programs:
- Agronomy -- sustainable agriculture
- Ecotourism -- sustainable tourism
- Veterinary Science -- animal production
- Nursing -- public health

### Cost of Living

Cost of living in Coroico is approximately **$500-800/month**, making it among the cheapest destinations in Latin America. Bolivia as a whole is ranked the 2nd cheapest country in the region for expats.

This low cost base is a strategic advantage for BARK -- it means the community can sustain itself at lower revenue thresholds while building out productive capacity.

### Market Access

**La Paz (3 hours)** is the primary market for Yungas agricultural products. The Mercado Rodriguez -- a 5 km-long daily market -- is the main distribution point.

Existing cooperatives like CENAPROC (founded 1992, organic/fair trade certified, Cup of Excellence winner) demonstrate that Yungas agricultural products can reach international specialty markets.

## Business Environment

What it takes to operate a business entity in Bolivia. The bureaucracy is real, but the cooperative structure offers meaningful advantages.

### Tax Structure

- **Corporate tax:** 25% on profits
- **Surtax:** Additional 25% if profits exceed threshold
- **VAT:** 13%
- Cooperatives classified as non-profit -- surplus reinvested or distributed per statute

### Bureaucratic Reality

Bolivia ranked approximately 150th on the World Bank's Doing Business index (now discontinued). Multiple registrations are required. Weak judicial security and complex regulations are documented concerns.

Foreigners can own businesses but face a more involved process. The 2014 Investment Law guarantees equal treatment for national and foreign firms, though public investment takes priority.

### Banking Access

Banking is limited for foreigners. The cooperative structure provides better access to the Bolivian financial system than a foreign-owned entity would.

**Banco de Desarrollo Productivo (BDP)** -- Bolivia's national development bank -- offers loans for agriculture, manufacturing, and renewable energy. It is also a Green Climate Fund Direct Access Entity, capable of channeling international climate finance.

### Cooperative Framework (Ley 356)

Bolivia's General Law of Cooperatives (Ley 356, 2013) provides the legal framework. Key features: non-profit classification, democratic governance (one member, one vote), autonomy from government, and regulatory oversight by AFCOOP. Registration requires formation documents, organic statutes, and socio-economic study -- submitted within 90 days of formation.

The cooperative tradition in Bolivia is rooted in ancestral Andean practices: *ayni* (reciprocity) and *ayllu* (community production). This is not an imported model -- it is indigenous to the culture.

## Foreign Organizations Operating in Bolivia

Others have navigated Bolivia's business environment successfully. Their approaches offer lessons for BARK.

### Practical Action Bolivia (UK NGO -- since ~1980s)

Adapted by hiring locally and working through Bolivian partner organizations. La Paz office. Reached 29,000 people in 2023. Delivered a 365% productivity boost through renewable energy for 4,300 farmers. Focus: regenerative agriculture and climate resilience.

### GIZ Bolivia (German Agency -- since 1962)

Long-term institutional presence. Programs in water/sanitation (benefiting 2M+ Bolivians), resource-saving irrigation (200,000+ rural people), and vocational training. Demonstrates that sustained foreign engagement works when aligned with local development priorities.

### Habitat for Humanity Bolivia (International NGO -- since 1985)

Three offices (Cochabamba, La Paz, Santa Cruz). Housing assistance, WASH programs targeting 76,570 people, women's land rights (365 women trained). Active for 40 years, demonstrating long-term foreign NGO viability.

### La Senda Verde (Wildlife Refuge -- since 2003)

Foreign-founded wildlife refuge and eco-lodge in Yolosa, 15 minutes from Coroico. Rescues 54+ species. Built treehouses from local sustainable cypress. Demonstrates that foreign-led eco-tourism ventures can operate successfully in the Yungas specifically.

### Documented Risks

- **2019 political crisis** affected foreign businesses across Bolivia
- **Road blockades** (bloqueos) routinely disrupt operations and supply chains
- **Currency controls** and dollar shortages created difficulties throughout 2024
- **No US bilateral investment treaty** since 2012

## Why BARK's Structure Works

BARK is designed as a cooperative, not a foreign-owned corporation. This is a strategic choice, not a compromise.

### Cultural Alignment

Cooperatives are not foreign to Bolivia -- they are indigenous. The Andean *ayllu* system of communal production and the *ayni* tradition of reciprocity predate Spanish colonization. BARK's cooperative structure aligns with deeply held cultural values, reducing friction that a corporate entity would face.

### Local Integration

BARK hires locally, buys locally, and trains locally. This is not corporate social responsibility theater -- it is how cooperatives function. Every member has a vote. The community is not extracting value; it is producing and sharing it. This model has established precedent: CENAPROC has been doing it since 1992.

### Government Alignment

Bolivia's $325M rural solar electrification program, IDB's $4.5B package, the World Bank's $125M rural electricity investment, and tourism development loans all create alignment with BARK's objectives. A cooperative that trains solar installers and processes local crops is exactly what these programs fund.

### Established Precedent

Small-scale agricultural processing cooperatives exist throughout the Yungas. CENAPROC produces award-winning organic coffee. La Senda Verde runs eco-tourism. FiBL/UMSA have 15 years of cacao research data. BARK is not inventing a model -- it is combining proven elements in a new way.
