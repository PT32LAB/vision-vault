# Bolivia Resilience: An Honest Analysis

*Why Bolivia during global uncertainty -- an honest, research-backed analysis of strengths, weaknesses, and what the Yungas specifically offers.*

## The Honest Verdict

Bolivia has genuine safety advantages during global crises -- geographic remoteness from conflict zones, no nuclear targets, low baseline violence, and indigenous community resilience structures that predate the state by centuries. These are not marketing claims. They are verifiable facts.

But the claim that Bolivia is "one of the safest places on Earth" requires heavy qualification. Its current economic collapse (reserves near $100M, 20%+ inflation, currency crisis), depleting energy sector, food import dependency, weak healthcare, and fragile infrastructure create vulnerabilities that could *amplify* -- not buffer -- the effects of a global crisis.

> Bolivia is a place where you are unlikely to be bombed, but where you might struggle to buy medicine, fuel, or flour. The Yungas specifically is better than urban Bolivia -- but it is not New Zealand.

This page lays out the full analysis -- strengths, weaknesses, scenario ratings -- so you can make your own judgment. All figures are sourced from UNODC, World Bank, IMF, and government reports. Where data is uncertain, we say so.

## What Bolivia Gets Right

Safety factors ranked from strongest to weakest. Evidence first, interpretation second.

### 1. No Nuclear or Strategic Military Value -- **Strong**

Zero nuclear weapons. Zero foreign military bases. Constitution explicitly prohibits them. Signatory to every major nuclear disarmament treaty (NPT 1970, TPNW 2019). Member of the Non-Aligned Movement -- no NATO, no mutual defense pacts. In nuclear war targeting models, Bolivia does not appear on any published target list.

Studies modeling nuclear winter food production identify South American countries as among the most likely to sustain civilization post-exchange. This is Bolivia's strongest card.

Sources: [OPANAL](https://opanal.org/en/bolivia/), [ICAN](https://www.icanw.org/bolivia), [Arms Control Association](https://www.armscontrol.org/act/2019-09/news-briefs/bolivia-ratifies-ban-treaty)

### 2. Geographic Remoteness from Conflict Zones -- **Strong**

Center of South America. Bordered by five countries, none engaged in or likely to enter a major international conflict. Thousands of kilometers from any active war zone, any great-power projection theater, any significant naval chokepoint.

South America as a whole has been interstate-war-free since the Cenepa War (Ecuador-Peru, 1995) -- making the entire continent one of the most peaceful regions on Earth for conventional warfare. Bolivia is insulated by multiple layers of neutral or friendly nations.

### 3. Low Baseline Violence -- **Moderate-Strong**

Bolivia's homicide rate of **4.1-4.3 per 100,000** (2023, estimates vary by source) is among the lowest in Latin America -- comparable to Chile and lower than the United States (6.4). During the 2019 political crisis, the overall annual rate actually *declined*. During COVID-19 (2020), it dropped to 3.49, the lowest recorded.

**Critical finding:** Crime does not spike catastrophically during crises. The 2019 political deaths (36 people) were concentrated in two specific incidents (Sacaba and Senkata massacres by security forces) -- not indicative of general social breakdown. Petty crime against foreigners remains a real concern, but Bolivia does not become Colombia when things get hard.

### 4. Institutional Resilience Through Political Drama -- **Moderate**

Bolivia has had a president ousted (2019), a coup attempt that collapsed in three hours (2024), a ruling party implosion from 60% to 3% (2025), and through all of it -- democratic elections continued, power transferred, and mass civil war never materialized. That is genuinely remarkable.

The counter-argument is real: tanks in the plaza is not a sign of healthy institutions. Bolivia has had over 190 coups, revolutions, and coup attempts since independence in 1825. The pattern is crisis, near-collapse, and recovery -- not stability. **Bolivia bends but does not break.** The relevant question: will it descend into Syria-style collapse? Almost certainly no. Will it be chaotic and uncomfortable? Almost certainly yes.

Sources: [2024 coup attempt (AS/COA)](https://www.as-coa.org/articles/reaction-bolivias-failed-coup-attempt-and-what-it-means), [AS/COA 2025 elections](https://www.as-coa.org/articles/explainer-bolivias-2025-elections)

### 5. Food Sovereignty Potential -- **Moderate**

"Food sovereignty" is in Bolivia's constitution since 2009. The reality is more complicated: Bolivia imports roughly 60% of its wheat, over 30% of fresh food, and aimed to be food self-sufficient by 2020 without achieving it. But the majority of domestically produced fresh food (estimates range from 80-90%, varying by source and definition) comes from small-scale family farms -- a decentralized food system that is harder to disrupt than industrial agriculture.

### 6. Indigenous Community Resilience & Ayllu System -- **Moderate**

The *ayllu* system -- traditional Andean community structure -- operates on reciprocal labor (*ayni/minka*), ecological complementarity, and collective resource management that predate the Spanish conquest by centuries. Communities must help neighbors with construction or farming; favors are returned. This creates a social safety net independent of money or the state.

**Caveat:** The ayllu system primarily operates in highland Andean communities (Quechua and Aymara). It is less relevant in the Yungas, which has Afro-Bolivian and mixed populations. For a foreigner, this advantage is real only to the extent they are integrated into local community structures.

Sources: [Ayllu (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ayllu), [FAO Bolivia](https://www.fao.org/social-protection/news-and-events/news/news-detail/fao-initiates-a-project-in-bolivia-to-strengthen-social-protection-for-indigenous-peoples/en)

## What Bolivia Gets Wrong

Vulnerability factors ranked from most to least serious. Ignoring these would be dishonest.

### Economic Collapse -- **Critical**

This is Bolivia's most severe current vulnerability. If a global crisis hits while Bolivia is already in economic freefall, the compound effect could be devastating.

| Metric | Value | Context |
|--------|-------|---------|
| Foreign Reserves | ~$100M | Down from $14B a decade ago |
| Inflation (2025) | 20%+ | Up from ~2% in 2022 |
| Credit Rating | CCC- | Near-default territory |
| Dollar Gap | 70-115% | Official vs. parallel rate |

Sources: [State Dept](https://www.state.gov/reports/2025-investment-climate-statements/bolivia/), [International Banker](https://internationalbanker.com/finance/dwindling-reserves-put-bolivias-economy-on-edge/), [IMF 2025](https://www.imf.org/en/news/articles/2025/05/30/pr-25168-bolivia-imf-concludes-2025-art-iv-consult)

### Healthcare System Weakness -- **Serious**

WHO ranking: 126th of 190 (year 2000 assessment, most recent available). Only 1.2 doctors per 1,000 people (global average: 2.6). During COVID-19, deaths in Bolivia's poorest departments were **7x higher than normal**. In the Yungas specifically, healthcare options are limited to local clinics -- serious medical emergencies require road evacuation to La Paz (3-4 hours). No helicopter services. No local hospitals with advanced care.

Sources: [Lancet (COVID Bolivia)](https://www.thelancet.com/journals/langlo/article/PIIS2214-109X(21)00001-2/fulltext), [Best-Country Healthcare](https://www.best-country.com/en/south_america/bolivia/medicine)

### Infrastructure Fragility -- **Serious**

The Yungas is reachable by one main road from La Paz. If that road is cut by a landslide or political blockade, the region is isolated. During the rainy season (November-March), convective storms exceeding 100mm in 24 hours trigger flash floods and landslides that frequently isolate communities. Road blockades during political protests regularly cut connections between regions -- this happened in 2019, 2024, and 2025.

### Energy Sector in Terminal Decline -- **Serious**

Gas production dropped from ~60 Mm3/day (2014 peak) to 31.9 Mm3/day (2024). Bolivia now imports 86% of its diesel and 54% of its gasoline -- spending $56M/week on fuel imports. Electricity generation is 67% natural gas. A fuel crisis is an everything crisis. During a global energy crisis, Bolivia would be competing for imported fuel with depleted reserves and a collapsing currency.

Sources: [US Trade.gov](https://www.trade.gov/country-commercial-guides/bolivia-energy), [FEE](https://fee.org/articles/running-on-empty/)

### Climate & Water Risk -- **Moderate**

The Tuni-Condoriri glaciers (water source for La Paz/El Alto) lost 39% of their area between 1983-2006. The Chacaltaya glacier has completely disappeared. In drought years, 84% of regional water comes from glacier melt. But -- and this matters -- **the Yungas is not glacier-dependent**. It receives abundant rainfall year-round (up to 1,350mm annually) with groundwater buffers. The climate risk is real for La Paz. It is significantly lower for the Yungas.

## Why Coroico, Not La Paz

The Yungas has micro-advantages that make it meaningfully better than urban Bolivia during a crisis. These are not theoretical -- they are geographic and agricultural facts.

- **Year-Round Food Production** -- Subtropical climate at 1,500-1,760m with consistent rainfall enables continuous growing. Coffee, citrus, coca, cotton, tropical fruits, root vegetables. No growing season interruption. When the altiplano freezes, the Yungas produces.
- **Water Abundance** -- Unlike La Paz/El Alto (glacier-dependent), the Yungas receives abundant rainfall throughout the year. Groundwater buffers mitigate dry-season severity. No glacier dependency. Water scarcity is not the concern here -- water quality and storage are.
- **Microclimate Diversity** -- The extreme altitude variation (1,000-4,650m along the road from La Paz) creates diverse microclimates supporting varied agriculture. You can grow temperate and tropical crops within a few kilometers of each other.
- **Peripheral to Political Violence** -- Political crises concentrate in La Paz, El Alto, Cochabamba, and Santa Cruz. The Yungas is peripheral to these conflicts. Road blockades can isolate it, but the violence itself stays in the cities.
- **Coca Economy Baseline** -- Legal coca cultivation provides guaranteed baseline income for Yungas farmers regardless of national economic conditions. The dried coca leaf market was valued at $303-374M (2017), representing 7-8.7% of agricultural GDP.
- **The Trade-Off** -- Single-road dependency. No serious healthcare. Suspicion toward outsiders in coca-growing areas. No wheat production. During a national crisis, the Yungas could be cut off from fuel, medical supplies, imported food, and communications simultaneously.

## The Scenario That Changes Everything

Most crisis comparisons assume normal conditions with one variable changed. The scenario that actually matters is the one where multiple systems fail simultaneously: **global air and sea logistics collapse, damage to digital infrastructure, and active war between historically warmongering nations** (US/China/Russia/NATO conflicts).

Under this compound scenario, the conventional hierarchy of safe-haven countries inverts. Islands become traps. NATO adjacency becomes a liability. Undersea cables become single points of failure. And the advantages of being landlocked, non-aligned, and already adapted to scarcity suddenly look different.

### New Zealand

Peacetime champion. Under compound stress, the picture shifts. New Zealand is a remote island nation that **imports 100% of its refined fuel** and depends entirely on sea logistics for manufactured goods, medicine, and industrial supplies. Its food production is massive but export-oriented -- converting to domestic consumption takes time and policy that may not arrive during a fast-moving crisis.

Digital infrastructure routes through undersea cables -- precisely the chokepoints damaged in this scenario. As a Five Eyes member (intelligence alliance with the US, UK, Australia, Canada), New Zealand is not neutral: it has intelligence-sharing agreements, hosts signals intelligence facilities, and could be targeted or blockaded in a US/China Pacific conflict.

**Compound scenario verdict:** Food-secure but fuel-dependent, digitally vulnerable via cable infrastructure, and politically aligned in ways that create targeting risk. An island fortress with no drawbridge.

### Uruguay

Excellent food producer, strong democratic institutions, no military alliances. But Uruguay's economy is structurally dependent on Argentina and Brazil -- its two giant neighbors. If those economies destabilize (likely in a global logistics collapse), Uruguay's trade routes, supply chains, and currency stability all suffer. Uruguay also depends on port access for virtually all non-regional trade.

**Compound scenario verdict:** Food-secure, non-aligned, but economically small and dependent on neighbors who would be in severe distress. Better than most, but its advantages are largely peacetime advantages.

### Paraguay

Similar structural advantages to Bolivia: landlocked (already adapted to non-maritime trade), agricultural economy, low cost of living, no military alliances. Paraguay is a major food producer (soybeans, beef) with genuine food self-sufficiency. But Paraguay's agriculture is industrialized and export-oriented -- heavily dependent on agrochemical imports and machinery. Its community resilience structures are weaker than Bolivia's indigenous networks.

**Compound scenario verdict:** Good structural position (landlocked, food-producing, non-aligned) but less community resilience and more dependent on industrial agriculture inputs that would become scarce.

### Bolivia (Yungas specifically)

Under this specific scenario, Bolivia's chronic weaknesses become paradoxical strengths. **Landlocked = already independent of sea logistics.** Bolivia's trade is 14% Brazil, 11% Argentina, 10% Peru -- all accessible by overland routes. It never had sea access, so losing it changes nothing. Its container costs are already 55.7% higher than Chile's; it has already adapted to that markup.

No military alliances. No intelligence-sharing agreements. No strategic bases. Constitution prohibits foreign military presence. In a US/China/Russia conflict, Bolivia is invisible -- no targeting value, no alliance obligations, no reason for any belligerent to notice it exists.

The Yungas specifically: year-round food production without industrial inputs (small-scale farming, not agrochemical-dependent monoculture). Abundant water independent of glaciers. Community structures that have survived 500 years of colonial and post-colonial disruption.

**The trade-offs remain real:** weak healthcare, economic crisis, fragile infrastructure, single-road dependency. Bolivia does not become Switzerland under stress. But it does not collapse the way maritime-dependent economies do when the ships stop coming.

### Compound Scenario Comparison: Logistics + Digital + War

| Factor | New Zealand | Uruguay | Paraguay | Bolivia (Yungas) |
|--------|-------------|---------|----------|-----------------|
| Food Security | High (surplus, but export-oriented -- conversion lag) | High (agricultural exporter) | High (but agrochemical-dependent) | Moderate-High in Yungas (year-round, low-input farming; wheat must be imported) |

| Comms Resilience | Vulnerable (undersea cable dependent; Starlink helps) | Moderate (overland fiber to neighbors; cable dependent for intercontinental) | Moderate (overland fiber to Brazil/Argentina) | Moderate (overland fiber to 5 neighbors; Starlink viable; no cable dependency) |
| Military Target Risk | Elevated (Five Eyes, intelligence facilities, Pacific alliance) | None (non-aligned, no bases) | None (non-aligned, no bases) | None (constitution prohibits foreign bases; non-aligned) |

| Economic Stability | Strong baseline, but import-dependent economy takes severe hit | Moderate (neighbor-dependent; suffers if Arg/Brazil collapse) | Moderate (informal economy absorbs shocks) | Already in crisis (CCC-); but subsistence economy partially decoupled from global finance |

**What the table shows:** Under this specific compound scenario, Bolivia's position improves relative to its peacetime ranking. New Zealand's greatest peacetime asset (island remoteness) becomes its greatest liability when ships stop coming. Bolivia's chronic weakness (landlocked, no sea access) becomes irrelevant -- because sea access is precisely what has collapsed.

Bolivia still loses on healthcare, economic stability, and energy. It wins on military target risk (zero), sea logistics dependency (zero), and community cohesion (centuries-deep). It ties or leads on communications resilience (overland fiber to five neighbors, no undersea cable dependency).

**The asymmetry is clear:** Bolivia's weaknesses are things a community can partially address (build solar, stockpile medicine, grow food). New Zealand's weakness under this scenario -- being an island when the sea routes are gone -- cannot be addressed at any scale.

## The Real Argument

> Staying with the trouble requires learning to be truly present, not as a vanishing pivot between awful or edenic pasts and apocalyptic or salvific futures, but as mortal critters entwined in myriad unfinished configurations of places, times, matters, meanings.
> -- Donna Haraway, *Staying with the Trouble*

BARK is not selling Bolivia as the safest place on Earth. The comparison table above makes that claim untenable. If safety is your only criterion, buy a house in New Zealand or Iceland and be done with it. What BARK argues is different.

The question is not where to *hide* but where to *build capability*. Resilience is not a geographic feature you consume. It is a practice you develop. A community that grows its own food, generates its own power, manages its own water, and governs itself does not need Bolivia to be safe -- it needs Bolivia to be *affordable enough and left-alone enough* to do the work.

Bolivia offers that. Land costs a fraction of Costa Rica. The legal framework recognizes cooperatives as first-class entities. The Yungas provides year-round food production and abundant water. The government, perpetually preoccupied with its own crises, largely leaves small communities to manage themselves. That is not a bug. For our purposes, it is the primary feature.

> Not a bunker. A workshop. Not a safe haven. A place where you can practice the skills that make any place safer.

Anna Tsing writes about life in the ruins of capitalism -- about the organisms and communities that flourish not in spite of disturbance but *through* it. Matsutake mushrooms growing in the wreckage of industrial forests. Collaborative survival in damaged landscapes. BARK takes this insight seriously: the world is not going to become safe. The question is how to live well and build capacity in a world that is already precarious.

Bolivia, with all its fractures and all its stubborn refusal to collapse entirely, is a place where that question can be answered with your hands in the soil.

## Who This Works For

**It works if you are:**
- Already embedded or willing to embed in a Yungas community with local relationships and Spanish fluency
- Earning external income (dollars, remote work) while living on Bolivia's low cost base
- Prioritizing distance from nuclear and military targets above other considerations
- Interested in building capability -- food production, energy independence, community governance -- not just consuming safety

**It does not work if you:**
- Depend on reliable healthcare access
- Depend on imported goods, stable currency, or functioning financial systems
- Cannot or will not build local community trust over time
- Need reliable infrastructure -- roads, electricity, communications -- as a given rather than something you build yourself
