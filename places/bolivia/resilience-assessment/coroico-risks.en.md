## Part 1: Location Safety

### Crime Data

Numbers first, narratives second. Bolivia's crime statistics tell a more nuanced story than travel advisories suggest.

#### Homicide Rate Comparison

| Country | Homicide Rate | Context |
|---------|--------------|---------|
| Ecuador | ~44.5/100K | Surged from 5.7 in 2018; most violent in South America in 2023 |
| Colombia | ~20-30/100K | Improved dramatically but still high |
| Mexico | ~20+/100K | High aggregate, varies enormously by state |
| United States | ~6.4/100K | For comparison -- higher than Bolivia |
| **Bolivia** | **4.3/100K** | **Relatively low for the region (535 total homicides, 2023)** |
| Peru | <10/100K | Lower end of spectrum |
| Chile | <5/100K | Among the safest in the region |

Sources: [World Bank](https://data.worldbank.org/indicator/VC.IHR.PSRC.P5?locations=BO), [Statista](https://www.statista.com/statistics/984859/homicide-rate-bolivia/), [InSight Crime 2024](https://insightcrime.org/news/insight-crime-2024-homicide-round-up/)

#### Coroico Specifically

**No municipality-level crime statistics are publicly available for Coroico.** Bolivia does not publish granular crime data by municipality the way Mexico or Colombia do. We state this honestly rather than presenting national statistics as if they apply to a specific town.

What we can say from available sources: Coroico is a small tourist town (~23,000 municipality population) frequently described as "quiet during the week" and a "weekend getaway for pacenos" (La Paz residents). Multiple travel sources describe it as having "extremely friendly people." It is listed among the "12 Best Places to Retire in Bolivia" by multiple outlets citing "ideal climate, scenic beauty, and low cost of living."

Petty crime exists as it does throughout Bolivia. Violent crime targeting foreigners in Coroico specifically has no documented pattern in available sources. La Paz city's crime indices (Crime Index: 60.13, Safety Index: 39.87 on Numbeo) should **not** be directly applied to Coroico -- urban crime patterns are largely absent in small Yungas towns.

### Travel Advisories

What governments say about traveling to Bolivia, and what that means in context.

#### United States -- Level 2: Exercise Increased Caution

Due to civil unrest. This is the same level as France, Germany, and the UK. It is **not** Level 3 (Reconsider Travel) or Level 4 (Do Not Travel).

The Chapare region is specifically designated "Do Not Travel" due to violent crime. **The Yungas is not.** It falls under the general Level 2.

Source: [US State Department, June 2024](https://travel.state.gov/content/travel/en/traveladvisories/traveladvisories/bolivia-travel-advisory.html)

#### United Kingdom -- General Caution

Advises caution with cameras and binoculars in coca-growing areas including the Yungas, "where people can be suspicious of anyone taking photographs."

Warns about road safety on the Death Road and road conditions during rainy season. **Does not advise against travel to Coroico or the Yungas.**

Source: [UK FCDO](https://www.gov.uk/foreign-travel-advice/bolivia/safety-and-security)

#### Canada -- Exercise High Degree of Caution

General advisory to avoid Chapare and Yungas "due to elevated crime" linked to coca production. Canada's advisory is the most cautious of the three regarding the Yungas specifically.

Context matters: Canada groups the entire Yungas with the Chapare, which is a significantly different risk profile. The Yungas is not the Chapare.

Source: [Canada Travel Advice](https://travel.gc.ca/destinations/bolivia)

### The Coca Economy -- Context, Not Controversy

The Yungas is one of Bolivia's two legal coca-growing regions. This is the primary reason for elevated travel advisories. Understanding the distinction between the Yungas and the Chapare is essential.

#### Yungas Coca
- Traditional, high-quality cultivation
- Primarily for domestic use: chewing and tea
- Legal and constitutionally protected in Bolivia
- Centuries of cultural tradition
- **Not** associated with international drug trade

#### Chapare Coca
- More recent, large-scale cultivation
- More associated with cocaine production
- Higher security force presence and conflict
- US "Do Not Travel" designation
- Different geographic region from Coroico

> Coca leaf is to cocaine as grape is to brandy. The leaf itself is mild and nutritious -- used for millennia as a stimulant, digestive aid, and sacred offering.

**BARK's position:** We respect local agricultural traditions entirely. Coca is deeply cultural in the Yungas. Disrespecting it would mean disrespecting the community we are joining. BARK does not grow coca but fully supports the rights of local communities to continue their traditional cultivation.

### Political Stability

Bolivia's political landscape is dynamic. Anyone planning to build something permanent here needs to understand the patterns.

**Recent History:** Bolivia has experienced significant political instability: the 2019 crisis following Evo Morales' resignation, the 2020 interim government, and the 2024 economic and political tensions. In August 2025, Bolivia elected a pro-market, pro-reform government that is seeking to restore international relations and pursue investor-friendly reforms. The trajectory is toward stability, but Bolivia's political culture is cyclical.

**Road Blockades (Bloqueos):** Road blockades are a deeply embedded Bolivian protest tradition. The La Paz-Coroico route gets blocked during national disputes. During the 2024 political crisis, blockades disrupted travel and supply chains across the country. Tourism took "a serious hit." **This is a real planning consideration, not an edge case.** Blockades can cut supplies for days. Any community in the Yungas needs a plan for this.

**Economic Context:** Bolivia is experiencing a prolonged economic crisis: severe fuel shortages, dollar scarcity, and inflation exceeding 20% as of 2025 (IMF). Foreign reserves have dropped to approximately $100M. The cooperative sector is constitutionally recognized and generally receives favorable treatment, but the broader economic environment is challenging. Foreign investment protections exist but are weaker than in neighboring countries -- Bolivia abrogated its bilateral investment treaty with the US in 2012.

### Natural Hazards

The Yungas is beautiful because of the same geography that creates these hazards. Understanding them is the first step to managing them.

**Landslides -- Primary hazard:** This is the number one natural hazard. The rainy season (November-March) saturates the steep Yungas slopes, causing frequent landslides. The old "Death Road" earned its name primarily from these. Road closures happen multiple times per rainy season and can last hours to days. BARK's site selection and building standards must account for slope stability and drainage.

**Road Closures -- Seasonal:** Related to landslides but worth its own entry. The La Paz-Coroico road (the "new road" opened in 2006/2007 to replace the Death Road) is significantly safer but still subject to closures from mudslides and rockfall during heavy rains. During closures, Coroico is effectively isolated from La Paz.

**Flooding -- Seasonal:** River flooding in lower valleys during heavy rains. Coroico town itself is elevated enough to avoid most flooding, but low-lying agricultural areas and roads near rivers are vulnerable. Site selection should account for flood plains and drainage patterns.

**Earthquakes -- Low risk:** Bolivia has moderate seismic activity. The Yungas is not in the highest-risk zone (that would be the western volcanic chain), but earthquakes can occur. Building standards should include basic seismic resilience without requiring full earthquake-proof construction.

**UV Radiation -- Daily:** Strong UV at 1,750m elevation with thin, clear mountain air. Not a dramatic hazard, but chronic exposure without protection causes real skin damage. Sunscreen, hats, and UV-protective clothing are daily essentials for outdoor work. This is especially important for people with lighter skin who are accustomed to lower elevations.

### BARK Security Measures

How BARK plans to address the realities described above. These are design decisions, not aspirations.

- **Food Reserves** -- On-site food production supplemented by stored reserves. During road blockades, the community can sustain itself independently for weeks. This is not optional -- it is a core infrastructure requirement.
- **Water Independence** -- Independent water collection, filtration, and storage systems. No dependency on municipal water supply. The Yungas receives 1,100-1,300mm of annual rainfall -- water scarcity is not the issue. Water quality and storage are.
- **Solar Power** -- Off-grid solar micro-grid with battery storage. No dependency on the national electricity grid. Power independence means lights, communications, and medical equipment function regardless of external conditions.
- **Satellite Communications** -- Starlink for internet, satellite phone for emergencies. Mesh network communication independent of cell towers. When roads are blocked and cell towers go down, BARK maintains contact with the outside world.
- **Community Watch** -- Community-based security awareness. Good relationships with neighbors and local authorities. The best security is a community where everyone knows everyone and looks out for each other -- supplemented by practical protocols.
- **Emergency Protocols** -- Written emergency protocols for medical evacuation, natural disasters, road closure, and political unrest. Dedicated vehicle maintained for emergency transport. First aid station staffed by the community health worker. Regular drills.

## Part 2: Project Risk Disclosure

We believe transparency about risk is a prerequisite for informed participation. This section is intentionally blunt.

### Execution Risks

BARK is an early-stage project with a small founding team and no guaranteed outcomes. The vision is ambitious and the execution depends on attracting the right people, securing funding, navigating foreign legal systems, and building physical infrastructure from scratch. Most ambitious projects fail.

- **Team risk** -- Two co-founders. Key person dependency is real. If either leaves, the project's direction could change significantly.
- **Funding risk** -- No institutional funding secured. Bootstrap phase. Dependent on early participants contributing capital and labor.
- **Execution risk** -- Building semi-autonomous infrastructure in a developing country is hard. Timelines will slip. Costs will exceed estimates.
- **Scale risk** -- The project needs to reach a critical mass of participants to be viable. If we can't attract enough people, some components won't be feasible.

### Financial Risks

Any capital contributed to BARK should be considered at risk. This is not an investment with guaranteed returns.

- **Capital at risk** -- Cooperative shares may have limited liquidity. There may be no secondary market for your membership share.
- **Currency risk** -- The Bolivian boliviano (BOB) fluctuates against major currencies. Operating costs are in BOB; income may be in USD/EUR.
- **No guaranteed returns** -- Cooperative membership is participation, not investment. Financial returns depend on the community's economic success.
- **Sunk cost** -- Physical infrastructure and land improvements may not be easily liquidated if the project dissolves.

### Community Risks

Intentional communities have a high failure rate. The majority do not survive their first five years. Common causes: governance conflicts, financial disagreements, and interpersonal friction.

- **Governance conflicts** -- Decision-making in community settings is hard. Consensus breaks down. Factions form.
- **Personality clashes** -- Living in close community amplifies interpersonal friction.
- **Vision drift** -- Over time, the community's priorities may shift away from the original vision.
- **Cultural integration** -- Foreigners building a community in Bolivia must navigate cultural differences with respect and humility.

### Legal Risks

Operating in Bolivia's legal system requires specialized knowledge and local legal counsel.

- **Land law complexity** -- Bolivian land law restricts foreign ownership and has specific rules for agricultural and rural land.
- **Cooperative governance** -- Ley 356 has specific requirements for governance, reporting, and member rights that must be followed precisely.
- **Regulatory changes** -- Laws can change. A future government could impose new restrictions on foreign participation.
- **Dispute resolution** -- Legal disputes in Bolivia can be slow and unpredictable.

### What We're Doing About It

Identifying risks without mitigation is just anxiety.

- **Project Risks** -- Expanding the founding team (reducing key-person dependency), maintaining everything as open source (the project can continue without any individual), phased development (each phase delivers standalone value).
- **Financial Risks** -- Conservative budgeting, phased investment (don't spend what you haven't raised), diversified income streams ([[technopark]], incubator, retreats), clear financial reporting to all members.
- **Location Risks** -- Emergency supply reserves, medical evacuation plan, Starlink for communications redundancy, relationships with local emergency services, building to seismic and landslide-resistant standards.
- **Community Risks** -- Studying instructive intentional communities (Auroville, Mondragon), designing conflict resolution mechanisms before they're needed, clear bylaws and governance frameworks, trial periods for new members.
- **Legal Risks** -- Engaging Bolivian legal counsel specializing in cooperative law, structuring ownership to comply with foreign ownership restrictions, maintaining relationships with local government, building community goodwill.

> We'd rather have ten people who understand the risks than a hundred who don't.
