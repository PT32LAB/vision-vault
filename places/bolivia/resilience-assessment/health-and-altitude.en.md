## Altitude: The First Question Everyone Asks

Coroico sits at **1,750 meters** (5,740 feet). This is moderate altitude -- comparable to Denver, Colorado or Bogota's suburbs. Most people adapt within 1-2 days with no symptoms at all.

The concern is not Coroico itself -- it's the journey. La Paz's El Alto International Airport sits at **4,061 meters** (13,323 feet), one of the highest commercial airports in the world. Visitors typically spend 1-2 nights acclimatizing in La Paz before descending to Coroico. Once you arrive, the subtropical air at 1,750m is noticeably easier to breathe than the Altiplano above.

| Location | Elevation | Note |

| Coroico | 1,750m | Most adapt in 1-2 days |
| La Paz city | 3,640m | Real altitude sickness territory |

## Medical Infrastructure

What exists now, what's nearby, and what the limitations are. We present this honestly because healthcare access is a critical factor in any relocation decision.

### In Coroico: Hospital de Coroico

Basic public hospital in town. Handles common illnesses, minor injuries, and basic maternity care. Limited capacity, limited equipment, limited specialist availability.

- Basic emergency care and stabilization
- Primary care consultations
- Basic laboratory and pharmacy
- **Limitation:** No surgery, no imaging (CT/MRI), no ICU

### Surrounding Area: Health Posts & Local Workforce

Postas de salud (health posts) serve surrounding rural communities with basic primary care. Carmen Pampa University, 12 km from Coroico, runs a five-year nursing program -- creating a local pipeline of trained health workers.

- Rural health posts for primary care in villages
- Carmen Pampa nursing graduates entering the local workforce
- Traditional medicine practitioners in indigenous communities
- **Limitation:** Limited dental care locally

### La Paz (3-3.5 hours): Full-Service Hospitals

For anything serious -- surgery, specialist consultations, imaging, complex diagnostics -- La Paz is the destination. The drive is 3-3.5 hours under normal conditions.

- **Hospital de Clinicas** -- public teaching hospital, largest in Bolivia
- **Clinica del Sur** -- private hospital, modern facilities, English-speaking staff
- Full range of specialists, surgery, imaging, and ICU
- **Risk:** Road closures during rainy season can cut access

### What BARK Will Add (Planned)

BARK's health infrastructure is designed to fill the gaps between what Coroico offers and what La Paz provides -- not to replace hospitals, but to handle the daily and the urgent.

- **Community health worker** -- one of the critical open positions
- On-site first aid station with supplies for common injuries and illnesses
- Telemedicine via Starlink -- remote consultations with specialists
- Emergency evacuation plan -- satellite phone, dedicated vehicle always available

## Preventive Health

The best health plan is not needing a hospital. BARK's design prioritizes prevention through environment, nutrition, and community.

- **Clean Water** -- Independent water systems with filtration and treatment. Waterborne illness is a leading cause of health problems in rural Bolivia -- BARK addresses this at the infrastructure level.
- **Nutrition** -- On-site food production means fresh vegetables, fruit, and protein year-round. The Yungas growing season never stops. Diet quality directly reduces chronic disease risk.
- **Active Lifestyle** -- Community life at 1,750m with daily physical work, walking terrain, and outdoor living. This is not a sedentary environment. The lifestyle itself is preventive medicine.
- **Mental Health** -- Community support structures, shared purpose, connection to nature, and retreat center practices. Isolation -- the silent killer in modern life -- is structurally impossible here.
- **Climate** -- Year-round 18-19 degrees C average. No extreme heat, no extreme cold. No heating or cooling needed. The subtropical highland climate is one of the most comfortable on Earth for human habitation.
- **Air Quality** -- Cloud forest air, far from industrial pollution. No factory emissions, no traffic congestion. No local air quality monitoring data exists for Coroico, but the absence of industrial and heavy traffic sources suggests low particulate matter compared to urban Bolivia.

## Honest Health Risks

No location is risk-free. These are the specific health hazards of the Coroico/Yungas area. We list them because informed people make better decisions.

### Tropical Diseases -- Moderate risk

Dengue fever and leishmaniasis are present in the lower Yungas. At 1,750m, Coroico has lower mosquito density than the lowland tropics, but risk is not zero. Dengue-carrying Aedes mosquitoes can survive at this elevation. Preventive measures: mosquito nets, repellent, standing water elimination, community vector control.

### Venomous Snake Bites -- Low but real risk

Fer-de-lance (Bothrops species) and coral snakes are present in the Yungas region. Bites are uncommon but can be serious. Mitigation: cleared paths, proper footwear, awareness training for all community members, antivenom in the first aid station, and emergency evacuation protocol to La Paz.

### Landslide Injuries -- Seasonal risk

The rainy season (November-March) brings landslide risk throughout the Yungas. This affects road travel primarily but can also damage structures on unstable slopes. BARK's site selection will prioritize geologically stable terrain. Building codes will account for soil saturation and slope angles.

### Limited Dental Care -- Ongoing gap

Dental services in Coroico are minimal. Serious dental work requires a trip to La Paz. Preventive dental care and regular check-ups before relocating are strongly recommended.

### Supply Chain Disruption -- Periodic risk

Road blockades (bloqueos) are a Bolivian protest tradition. When the La Paz-Coroico route is blocked, medication and medical supply chains are disrupted. This can last hours to days. BARK mitigation: maintain a 3-month buffer of essential medications and medical supplies on-site at all times.

### UV Radiation -- Daily consideration

At 1,750m with clear mountain air, UV radiation is significantly stronger than at sea level. Sunburn and long-term skin damage are real concerns for outdoor work. Sunscreen (SPF 30+), hats, and UV-protective clothing are daily essentials, not optional accessories.

## Healthcare Reality Today

What actually exists right now in the Coroico area. No projections -- just current facts.

### What Exists in Coroico
- **Hospital de Coroico** -- basic public hospital. Primary care, minor injuries, basic maternity. No surgery, no imaging, no ICU.
- Rural health posts (postas de salud) in surrounding villages.
- Carmen Pampa University (12 km) runs a nursing program -- producing local health workers.
- Traditional medicine practitioners in indigenous communities.
- **For anything serious** -- surgery, specialists, imaging -- La Paz (3-3.5 hours by road). Road can be cut by landslides in rainy season.

### Environmental Health Factors
- 1,750m altitude -- moderate; some studies associate this range with lower cardiovascular disease rates, though evidence is mixed and confounders are numerous.
- 18-19 degrees C year-round -- minimal thermoregulatory stress.
- No industrial pollution sources locally, though no formal air quality monitoring data exists for Coroico.
- Year-round growing season supports locally sourced nutrition.

*Long-Term Vision*

The Yungas environment has characteristics that could be favorable for longitudinal health studies: consistent mild climate reducing seasonal confounders, access to locally grown food, and a community context that supports long-term participant retention. This is a Phase 3+ requirement -- not relevant to current retreat operations but essential for the longevity research vision.

## What to Bring

Practical preparation for anyone considering a stay or relocation.

1. **Medications** -- Personal medication supply with a **3-month buffer minimum**. Prescription medications may not be available locally or may be different formulations. Bring documentation of prescriptions in English and Spanish.

2. **Insurance** -- Comprehensive travel/health insurance that **explicitly covers Bolivia**. Include medical evacuation coverage. Verify that your policy covers evacuation from rural areas and does not exclude pre-existing conditions you may have.

3. **Vaccinations** -- **Yellow fever vaccination** is recommended for the Yungas region. Ensure routine vaccinations (tetanus, hepatitis A/B, typhoid) are current. Bring your vaccination records -- some border crossings may request them.

4. **First Aid** -- Personal first aid kit for minor injuries. Quality sunscreen (SPF 30+), insect repellent with DEET or picaridin, and any allergy medications. Sturdy footwear for terrain that includes steep, muddy paths.
