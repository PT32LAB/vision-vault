## What We Have Learned So Far

Key findings from 16 research documents, 449 verified links, and a rigorous fact-check of our own claims. This is what we know, what we do not know, and what surprised us.

### The legal path exists, but has constraints

Bolivia's Ley 356 allows foreigners to be cooperative members (Art. 33), and the constitution grants foreigners equal rights (Art. 14). However, Art. 65 requires Bolivian *citizenship* for council and leadership positions. Cooperatives are subject to IUE (25%), IVA (13%), and IT (3%) taxes -- they are not tax-free. Only mining cooperatives get near-zero taxation. A Bolivian co-founder is not optional. It is a legal requirement for leadership.

### Bolivia is safer than its reputation suggests -- with caveats

Bolivia's homicide rate is 4.32 per 100,000 -- among the lowest in Latin America, comparable to Chile. During the 2019 political crisis, the annual homicide rate actually declined. Coroico specifically is described by visitors and travel guides as quiet and friendly. The Yungas has a Level 2 (not "Do Not Travel") advisory from the US State Department. However: no municipality-level crime data exists for Coroico, Bolivia's current economic crisis (reserves near $100M, 20%+ inflation) could change the picture, and the coca factor means some governments advise caution in the region.

### Foreigners have built successful projects in the Yungas

Gravity Bolivia (New Zealand founder, 1998) pioneered Death Road mountain biking and still operates 28 years later. La Senda Verde (2003) runs a wildlife sanctuary and eco-lodge in Yolosa, near Coroico, using a hybrid NGO/eco-lodge model with international volunteers. El Ceibo Cooperative (1,200+ cacao families) used Inter-American Foundation grants to industrialize and went self-sufficient over 16 years. Practical Action, GIZ, Habitat for Humanity, and Engineers Without Borders all have active programs in Bolivia. The pattern: succeed by aligning with Bolivia's national priorities, partnering with indigenous communities, and framing work as capacity-building -- not as outsiders coming to "save" anyone.

### The economics are plausible but unproven

Conservative modeling of five processing lines (coffee, cacao, fruit dehydration, essential oils, solar installation) projects combined net income of $43K-$129K/year on $65K-$158K total investment. Coffee has the fastest ROI (1.5-3 years) and benefits from the Yungas producing 95% of Bolivia's arabica. Solar dehydration has the lowest entry cost ($2K-$8K) and fastest break-even (3-12 months). These are spreadsheet numbers. They assume finding buyers, maintaining quality, navigating Bolivian logistics, and not encountering any of the thousand things that can go wrong. We need Phase 1 ground-truth data before treating any of these as reliable.

### Our own website had claims that needed correction

A rigorous fact-check of our core pages found that the road from La Paz drops ~1,890m (not "3,400m" as we stated -- the larger figure is only accurate from the highest pass above La Paz). Several page descriptions used present tense for things that do not exist yet. The claim "open-source plans" had no public GitHub repos to back it up. The temperature range "18-28C" is accurate for daytime but omits nighttime lows of 12-15C in winter. We have corrected what we can and flagged the rest. Being wrong about our own facts, and admitting it, is part of the process.

### Bolivia's economic crisis is real and getting worse

Foreign reserves are near $100M (critically low), inflation exceeds 20%, and a currency crisis is unfolding. Energy infrastructure is deteriorating and the country imports food (especially wheat). These conditions could either make BARK's self-sufficiency model more relevant -- or make operating in Bolivia significantly harder. This is not a stable environment to build in. It is an environment where resilient systems are needed most, but also where they are hardest to establish.

### The biggest unknown: what we have not seen with our own eyes

No amount of research replaces a site visit. We do not know the real state of roads in rainy season. We do not know what the power grid is actually like. We do not know how community leaders will respond to our proposal. We do not know what land is actually available and at what price. Phase 1 exists entirely to answer these questions. Everything before it is educated guessing.
