# Participation Means Real Involvement

BARK is not asking for passive belief. It is asking for people who want to test whether they can build, contribute, live, and learn inside a serious long-term project.

![The green valley of the Yungas, Coroico](/images/yungas/valley.jpg)

Participation here can begin lightly, but it should never remain vague. The project needs presence, skill, commitment, and honesty about fit. Not everyone belongs here -- and that is not a failure. It is a filter that protects both the project and the person.

---

## Five Pathways

Five ways in -- each with real expectations.

### 01 -- Experience

Come for a retreat -- one week minimum. Live on-site, eat from the gardens, meet the builders, understand the systems. This is not tourism. You will work. You will be uncomfortable at least once. Some guests return. Some invest. Some relocate. Most do not. The retreat exists to find the ones who do.

- Structured retreat program (minimum 7 days)
- Work alongside residents -- not observe from a distance
- Honest assessment at the end: mutual fit or mutual goodbye

### 02 -- Invest

Put capital into land, solar arrays, processing equipment, or the cooperative structure itself. Every dollar becomes a physical thing in a specific place -- a cistern, a roasting drum, a panel on a roof. You will receive quarterly reporting on what your capital became. Not a pitch deck. Photographs and spreadsheets.

- Direct project funding with named asset allocation
- Cooperative membership shares (one member, one vote)
- Component-specific sponsorship with impact reporting

### 03 -- Build

Bring a skill that has weight. Weld a frame, design a greywater system, write firmware for a mesh radio node, plant a food forest on a 30-degree slope. We need people who have built things before -- not people who want to learn by experimenting on critical [[infrastructure]]. On-site in Coroico or remote.

- Resident builder -- relocate to Coroico, minimum 6-month commitment
- Remote contributor -- code, design, research with defined deliverables
- Visiting expert -- periodic on-site work on specific problems

See also: [[builders]]

### 04 -- Think

Contribute governance models, legal architecture, engineering designs, research. The knowledge base is open-source -- your work propagates to every community that forks the model. We need people who can write a document that survives contact with Bolivian administrative reality, not just theory.

- Governance model design grounded in Ley 356
- Technical architecture with material and cost specificity
- Research that cites sources and checks assumptions

### 05 -- Spread

Translate content into Spanish, Aymara, Portuguese. Connect us with cooperatives, agronomists, engineers, lawyers who know Bolivian land title law. Write honestly about the project -- including what does not work. Propaganda does not help us. Honest signal does.

- Translation with cultural adaptation, not word-for-word conversion
- Introductions to specific people who solve specific problems
- Honest public writing -- criticism included

## What a Good Fit Looks Like

The people who do well here share a few traits. Not credentials -- dispositions.

**You want real work.** You have built things before and you want to build again. You are not looking for a community that validates your presence -- you are looking for one that needs your output.

**You tolerate friction.** Uncertainty, unfinished systems, changing plans, and the gap between intention and reality do not paralyze you. You adapt. You communicate when something is not working instead of withdrawing.

**You contribute, not consume.** You are drawn to the project because of what you can put into it, not because of the atmosphere it might provide. Community is a byproduct of shared labor -- not a service.

---

## What This Is Not

Saying no early saves everyone time. If any of the following apply, this project is probably not a good fit -- and that is fine. There are many good projects in the world.

**Passive Retreat Tourism.** This is not an eco-resort with a membership fee. Living here means participating in governance, contributing labor, eating what the garden produces in season, and accepting that the road washes out in February.

**Instant Belonging.** Community forms through shared work over time. There is no initiation ceremony that makes you part of this. There is only sustained contribution and mutual trust built slowly.

**Polished Service.** Bolivia has fuel shortages, dollar scarcity, road blockades, and a political landscape that does not conform to North American or European expectations. Infrastructure here is functional, not luxurious.

**Low-Friction Living.** If you cannot work within Bolivian reality -- respectfully, patiently, without trying to import your own governance assumptions -- you will be unhappy here, and you will make others unhappy.

## What Happens After Contact

### 01 -- You Write

Send an email. Tell us what you do, what you have built, and what draws you here. No form. No application portal. Just a real message.

### 02 -- We Respond

Within one week. If we do not reply, follow up -- small teams lose emails sometimes. Silence is not rejection.

### 03 -- First Conversation

A video call. We talk about your background, your interests, and whether the project matches what you are looking for. Honest in both directions.

### 04 -- Mutual Evaluation

We decide together whether to proceed -- a visit, a remote contribution, an investment conversation. Fit is evaluated by both sides, not assigned.
