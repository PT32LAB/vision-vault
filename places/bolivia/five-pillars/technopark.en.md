# Techno Park

**Open-source R&D lab and maker space.**

The Techno Park is BARK's innovation engine — a space where appropriate technology is researched, prototyped, documented, and shared openly. Everything built here is open-source by default. Below is real equipment data with verified price ranges for the machinery that will form the Techno Park's core capabilities.

## Processing Equipment (Real Prices)

- **Coffee Wet Mill** — Depulper, fermentation tanks, washing channels, and drying beds. Small community-scale wet mill: **$1,500--$12,000** depending on capacity (500--5,000 kg/day). Essential for processing Yungas cherry to parchment coffee on-site rather than selling raw cherry at a fraction of the price.[^1]
- **Coffee Roaster** — Production-grade drum roaster for specialty coffee. Range: **$12,000--$40,000** for 5--30 kg batch capacity. Converts green coffee into roasted retail product ($25--45/lb) — see [[trading-house]] for current FOB pricing. The single highest ROI piece of equipment in the Techno Park.[^1]
- **Cacao Bean-to-Bar Line** — Roaster, winnower, grinder/conche, tempering machine, and molder. Small-batch (10--50 kg/day): **$5,000--$25,000**. Production-scale: **$50,000--$100,000**. Transforms raw cacao beans into finished chocolate bars retailing at $8--15 per 70g bar.[^2]
- **Solar Dehydrators** — For drying fruits, herbs, coffee, and cacao. DIY solar tunnel dryer: **$50--$200** in materials. Commercial stainless steel cabinet dryer: **$900--$1,900**. Critical for preserving Yungas tropical produce for export and long-term storage.[^3]
- **Essential Oil Distillery** — Steam distillation setup for extracting oils from aromatic plants. Copper or stainless steel still (20--100 liter): **$2,000--$3,600**. Yungas biodiversity provides raw materials; single-origin essential oils command $15--50 per 10ml at retail.[^4]

## Fabrication & Making

- **3D Printing Workshop** — FDM and resin printers for prototyping parts, tools, and replacement components. Capable setup: **$2,000--$10,000** for multiple printers covering different materials and resolutions. Critical for a remote community where ordering replacement parts means weeks of shipping. Print what you need.[^5]
- **Open Source Ecology's GVCS** — The [Global Village Construction Set](https://www.opensourceecology.org/) is an open-source project developing 50 industrial machines needed to build a small civilization with modern comforts. Includes: compressed earth brick press, tractor, sawmill, 3D printer, CNC torch table, and more. All designs are freely available. Several machines are directly buildable in the Techno Park using local materials and fabrication.[^6]
- **Fab Lab Concept** — Based on MIT's Fab Lab model, now with **2,500+ labs in 100+ countries** worldwide. A standard Fab Lab includes CNC router, laser cutter, 3D printer, electronics workbench, and vinyl cutter — total cost approximately $50,000--$100,000 for a full setup. The [Fab City](https://fab.city/) network provides design files, training curricula, and a global community of fabricators. BARK's Techno Park aims to be a Fab Lab node.[^7]

## Infrastructure Tech

- **Solar arrays** — 3.5--4.5 kWh/m²/day in the Yungas. Panels, inverters, and battery storage for community power independence.
- **Micro-hydro systems** — Excellent potential given Yungas terrain and rainfall. 1--100 kW systems using the abundant streams and elevation drop. Complements solar during cloudy/rainy periods.
- **Water purification** — Gravity-fed sand filtration, UV treatment, and ceramic filters. All maintainable with local materials and skills.
- **Mesh communications** — LoRa mesh network for community-wide connectivity independent of cellular infrastructure. Combined with Starlink for internet backbone.
- **Agricultural automation** — Soil moisture sensors, automated drip irrigation, greenhouse climate control. Low-cost IoT systems using ESP32 microcontrollers and open-source firmware.

> **Total Techno Park Equipment Budget Estimate:** Core processing equipment (coffee, cacao, oils, dehydration) plus basic Fab Lab setup: approximately **$80,000--$200,000** depending on scale and whether equipment is built on-site from open-source designs or purchased commercially. This is a fraction of comparable facilities in developed countries.

## How It Works

Resident makers and visiting researchers collaborate in shared workshop spaces. Every project is documented as open-source — plans, materials lists, build guides, test results. If BARK discovers a better way to purify water or generate power, that knowledge belongs to everyone.

## DIY Ethos

We don't buy what we can build. We don't hoard what we can share. The Techno Park embodies the principle that technology sovereignty — the ability to understand, maintain, and improve your own systems — is a prerequisite for genuine autonomy. Open Source Ecology's GVCS, the Fab Lab network, and Earthship Biotecture's building techniques all prove this is practical, not idealistic.

## Civilization Maintenance

This is the core concept behind the Techno Park — and arguably the most important idea on this entire website. The question is not "can we buy everything we need?" but **"what is the minimum technology stack required to maintain a modern quality of life, and can we build and repair it ourselves?"**

Six capabilities define the boundary between a community that survives and one that thrives: **electricity, clean water, communications, basic medical, food production, and fabrication.** If you can maintain all six from locally available materials and knowledge, you have chosen your civilization level — and no supply chain disruption, political crisis, or economic collapse can take it away.

| Capability | Technology | Self-Maintainable? |
|---|---|---|
| **Electricity** | Solar PV + micro-hydro + battery bank | Panels last 25+ years. Micro-hydro turbines repairable with basic metalworking. Nickel-iron batteries last 50+ years. |
| **Clean Water** | Gravity-fed sand filtration + UV + ceramic filters | 100% maintainable with local sand, gravel, and clay. Proven for 200+ years. |
| **Communications** | LoRa mesh + HF/VHF radio + Starlink | Mesh radio from ESP32 modules. Ham radio buildable from salvaged components. Starlink while available. |
| **Basic Medical** | Diagnostic equipment + surgical kit + pharmacy | Mechanical instruments last indefinitely. Medicines need stockpiling with rotation. Bolivia has 5,000+ studied medicinal plant species nationally. |
| **Food Production** | Permaculture food forest + seed bank + irrigation | 100% self-sustaining once established. Year-round growing in the Yungas. Never use hybrid seeds that don't breed true. |
| **Fabrication** | Forge + foundry + lathe + 3D printers + plastic recycling | The forge builds the forge. RepRap self-replicates 50-70% of parts by weight. Precious Plastic closes the loop on waste. |

**This is NOT survivalism.** This is engineering capability — the ability to maintain your chosen civilization level regardless of supply chain status. Cuba, Venezuela, rural Africa, and post-Soviet states have all demonstrated that communities survive disruption through fabrication capability, not stockpiles.

### Dave Gingery's Bootstrap Sequence

David J. Gingery (1932-2004) wrote a seven-book series proving you can build a complete machine shop from literal scrap metal. The sequence is a build chain where each machine enables the next:

- **Book 1: The Charcoal Foundry** ($0--$500 in scrap) — Melts aluminum from automotive parts using a firebrick foundry and a blower salvaged from a clothes dryer.
- **Book 2: The Metal Lathe** — Cast from the foundry. The lathe refines its own parts — it literally helps build itself.
- **Book 3: The Metal Shaper** — Built using lathe and foundry. Produces flat surfaces the lathe cannot.
- **Book 4: The Milling Machine** — Built using all prior machines. Precision surfaces, slots, keyways.
- **Book 5: The Drill Press** — Precision hole-making beyond hand drill capability.
- **Book 6: The Dividing Head** — Enables cutting gears and splines. Turns a workshop into one that can build complex mechanisms.
- **Book 7: The Sheet Metal Brake** — Bending and forming for enclosures, guards, and structural components.

Total cost: approximately $50 for the book set. Raw materials: effectively free (scrap metal + charcoal). The result: a complete machine shop capable of making precision metal parts, including parts for more tools.

### Precious Plastic: Open-Source Recycling

[Precious Plastic](https://www.preciousplastic.com/) by Dave Hakkens provides open-source designs for four machines that create a closed-loop plastic recycling system: **shredder** (grinds waste into flakes), **extrusion machine** (melts flakes into continuous profiles and filament), **injection molder** (specific parts from molds), and **sheet press** (flat sheets from compressed flakes). All designs are downloadable. DIY build cost: $200--$2,000 per machine. Combined with 3D printing, this turns plastic waste into raw material for tools, water pipe, containers, greenhouse panels, and replacement parts.

### The Scavenger-Ready Philosophy

In any disruption scenario — economic collapse, supply chain failure, natural disaster, political isolation — a community's survival depends on its ability to: **salvage** useful components from broken technology (stepper motors from printers, alternators from vehicles, copper from motors, neodymium magnets from hard drives), **process** scavenged materials into usable forms (melt aluminum in a charcoal foundry, sort steel by spark test, strip copper wire, shred and extrude plastics), and **fabricate** replacement parts and new tools from processed materials.

The key capability chain:

- **Scavenged metal** -> Foundry/Forge -> Cast/forged parts -> Machines
- **Scavenged plastic** -> Shredder -> Recycled flake -> Extruded/molded products
- **Scavenged electronics** -> Disassembly -> Components -> New circuits/generators

> **The library is the most important infrastructure.** Tools without knowledge are scrap metal. A curated collection of technical books and reference materials — stored digitally on solar-powered devices and as physical books that require no electricity — is the single highest-value investment for long-term community resilience.

## Self-Replicating Workshop

Beyond processing equipment and fabrication tools, the Techno Park aims to build something more fundamental: a self-replicating workshop — a set of tools that can reproduce and repair themselves. This is not science fiction. Dave Gingery's seven-book series demonstrates how to build a complete machine shop starting from nothing but scrap metal and a charcoal foundry. The sequence begins with casting a lathe bed from aluminum cans, then using that lathe to build a shaper, then a milling machine, then a drill press, and so on. Total cost for the starting foundry: $0--$500 in scrap materials. The result: a workshop capable of making precision metal parts — including parts for more tools.

The [RepRap project](https://reprap.org/) took this idea into the digital domain. RepRap is a self-replicating 3D printer — a machine that can print the majority of its own components. The project has achieved approximately [50--70% self-replication by component weight](https://reprap.org/wiki/RepRap), meaning a working RepRap can produce the majority of the parts needed to build another RepRap. Combined with the Gingery approach for metal components, this creates a bootstrapping path from scrap materials to a fully equipped fabrication shop.

Open Source Ecology's Global Village Construction Set (GVCS) scales this concept to civilization level: 50 open-source machines — from tractors to circuit makers — designed so that communities can build and maintain their own industrial infrastructure. All designs are freely available, and several machines are directly buildable from materials available in the Yungas.

The "scavenger-ready" concept ties these threads together. In a region where Amazon deliveries don't arrive and replacement parts take weeks by bus, the ability to salvage components from broken electronics, vehicles, and machinery — and fabricate replacements — is not a hobby, it's a survival skill. Precious Plastic, an open-source project providing designs for plastic recycling machines (shredder, extruder, injection molder, sheet press), adds another dimension: turning plastic waste into raw material for new parts and products.

Minimum Viable Workshop cost: $700--$3,000. That gets you a Gingery-style foundry, a basic RepRap printer, hand tools, and the knowledge base to start building everything else. The goal is not to buy a workshop — it's to grow one.
